## Physics Calculation

### Random 64-bit FPR (baseline: two truly random fingerprints)

For two independent random 64-bit strings, P(Hamming(A,B) <= k) = sum_{i=0}^{k} C(64,i) / 2^64.

| k | Hamming volume | FPR (truly random) |
|---|---|---|
| k=0 | 1 | 5.42e-20 |
| k=1 | 65 | 3.52e-18 |
| k=2 | 2,081 | 1.13e-16 |
| k=3 | 43,745 | 2.37e-15 |
| k=4 | 679,121 | 3.68e-14 |
| k=5 | 8,303,633 | 4.50e-13 |

At k=2: FPR = 1.13e-16. At k=3: FPR = 2.37e-15. The k=3 FPR is 21x higher than k=2, but both are negligibly small — the expected number of false-positive pairs in a 22,381-memory corpus is 0.000000 (k=2) and 0.000001 (k=3). This is not the bottleneck.

### SimHash FPR for Dissimilar Content

SimHash is not random — it preserves locality. For two documents with cosine similarity cos(theta), P(bit j agrees) = 1 - theta/pi (Charikar 2002, the fundamental SimHash bit-agreement theorem). The Hamming distance between two SimHash fingerprints is then Binomial(64, 1 - p_agree).

|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0.00 (unrelated) | 0.5000 | 32.00 | 1.13e-16 | 2.37e-15 |
| 0.05 | 0.5159 | 30.98 | 7.40e-16 | 1.46e-14 |
| 0.10 | 0.5319 | 29.96 | 4.59e-15 | 8.51e-14 |
| 0.20 | 0.5641 | 27.90 | 1.53e-13 | 2.50e-12 |
| 0.25 | 0.5804 | 26.85 | 8.35e-13 | 1.28e-11 |
| 0.30 | 0.5970 | 25.79 | 4.42e-12 | 6.32e-11 |
| 0.50 | 0.6667 | 21.33 | 2.89e-09 | 3.09e-08 |
| 0.70 | 0.7468 | 16.20 | 1.96e-06 | 1.44e-05 |
| 0.80 | 0.7952 | 13.11 | 6.44e-05 | 3.68e-04 |
| 0.90 | 0.8564 | 9.19 | 3.37e-03 | 1.30e-02 |
| 0.95 | 0.8989 | 6.47 | 3.68e-02 | 1.01e-01 |
| 0.99 | 0.9549 | 2.88 | 4.45e-01 | 6.74e-01 |
| 0.999 | 0.9841 | 1.02 | 9.37e-01 | 9.87e-01 |

**Critical observation**: For short memories with shared stop words (cosine ~0.25, 50-char content), P(Hamming<=2) = 8.35e-13 and P(Hamming<=3) = 1.28e-11. Expected FP pairs at 22K corpus: 0.00 in both cases. FPR is not the problem.

### The Real Problem: Detection Rate (Sensitivity)

The k=2 vs k=3 framing asks the wrong question. The physics shows that at k=2 AND k=3, the false-positive rate is effectively zero across all content lengths. What actually determines system behavior is the **true positive rate** — how reliably SimHash detects actual near-duplicates.

For Recall's near-duplicate definition (same information stored multiple times), the relevant similarity ranges are:

| Memory type | Typical cosine | E[Hamming] | P(Ham<=2) | P(Ham<=3) | P(Ham<=10) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Same hook fires twice (byte-identical) | 1.000 | 0.00 | 100.0% | 100.0% | 100.0% |
| Minor whitespace/punctuation difference | 0.999 | 0.91 | 93.7% | 98.7% | 100.0% |
| Same fact, mostly same words | 0.990 | 2.88 | 44.5% | 67.4% | 100.0% |
| Paraphrase, same key terms | 0.950 | 6.47 | 3.7% | 10.1% | 94.5% |
| Same topic, different phrasing | 0.900 | 9.19 | 0.3% | 1.3% | 69.2% |
| Related memories, different details | 0.800 | 13.11 | 0.0% | 0.0% | 21.3% |

**k=2 and k=3 both fail to catch the majority of near-duplicates at cosine < 0.999.** At cosine=0.99 (same fact, mostly same words), k=2 misses 55% and k=3 misses 33% of actual near-duplicate pairs. To achieve 80% detection at cosine=0.99 you need k=6. To achieve 80% detection at cosine=0.95 you need k=8.

Minimum k for 80% detection threshold at each similarity level:

|---|---|

| cosine >= 0.9936 | k=3 |
| cosine >= 0.9814 | k=5 |
| cosine >= 0.9493 | k=8 |
| cosine >= 0.9185 | k=10 |

### Content Length and Token Count: The SNR Analysis

SimHash bit quality depends on the SNR of the bit accumulator. For a near-duplicate pair with 90% token overlap (cosine=0.90), the bit accumulator has:

- Mean signal per bit = N_tokens × (2 × p_agree - 1) = N_tokens × 0.567
- Standard deviation = sqrt(N_tokens) (unit weights, independent tokens)
- SNR = N_tokens × 0.567 / sqrt(N_tokens) = 0.567 × sqrt(N_tokens)

| N tokens | SNR | E[Hamming] (near-dup) | P(Ham<=2) | P(Ham<=3) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5 (~25 chars) | 1.59 | 3.55 | 30.4% | 52.3% |
| 8 (~40 chars) | 2.02 | 1.40 | 83.5% | 94.8% |
| 10 (~50 chars) | 2.25 | 0.77 | 95.7% | 99.2% |
| 15 (~75 chars) | 2.76 | 0.18 | 99.9% | 100.0% |
| 20 (~100 chars) | 3.19 | 0.05 | 100.0% | 100.0% |

**Wait — this appears to contradict the above.** The SNR analysis shows that for small token counts, SimHash fingerprints of near-duplicates (90% overlap) CONVERGE faster than expected: at 8 tokens they already have only 1.4 expected Hamming distance. This is because the SNR model computes the variance of the bit accumulator, and for a high-overlap pair (90%), the signal is strong even with few tokens.

The resolution: the two analyses address different things. The SNR analysis shows how reliably the **fingerprint converges to the theoretical value** (where theory predicts E[Hamming]=9.19 for 90% overlap). For short token sets, the realized Hamming distance for a specific near-duplicate pair fluctuates — it can be low (detectable) or high (missed). The asymptotic (large N) theoretical value E[Hamming]=9.19 only applies when the SNR is high enough that the fingerprint has converged. At low N, the realized fingerprint is noisy and any individual pair could have any Hamming distance.

**The practical content length threshold conclusion**: SimHash fingerprints are reliable (low variance) for near-duplicate detection when N_tokens >= 15, corresponding to approximately 75-100 characters for English prose. Below this threshold, the fingerprint is highly variable across multiple writes of the "same" near-duplicate content. The right tool for sub-75-char memories is not k adjustment but a different mechanism (Levenshtein distance or exact-match hashing with normalization variants).

### Physics Ceiling and Current Gap

**The theoretical minimum FPR** for 64-bit SimHash at k=2 on unrelated content is 1.13e-16 (the random-fingerprint bound). The current proposed implementation achieves this floor — there is no FPR gap. SimHash's locality-preserving property means dissimilar content (cosine < 0.3) has even lower FPR than random fingerprints, and for fully unrelated content (cosine ~0), FPR equals the random bound exactly.

**The actual gap** is in detection rate. For memories with cosine 0.90-0.99 (which Q013 estimates as a significant fraction of the redundancy problem), k=2 and k=3 both provide essentially no coverage. k=11 is needed for 80% detection at cosine=0.90. k=6 is needed for 80% detection at cosine=0.99. Neither is what Q141 recommended.

**Gap source**: Q141 imported Google's k=3 calibration without accounting for the fact that Google's k=3 was calibrated on multi-billion-page corpora where "near-duplicate" = pages with >98% token overlap (boilerplate additions to scraped content), not 90% overlap. For Google's operating point (cosine ~0.99+), k=3 achieves 67-99% detection. For Recall's operating point (the broad near-duplicate range 0.85-0.99), k=3 is systematically miscalibrated.

## Qualifying Ideas

**simhash-k-calibration-by-similarity-tier**
(N=0.72, E=0.82, F=0.85)

The correct design is not a single k value but a **tiered k strategy** matched to Recall's near-duplicate tiers:

- Tier 0 (byte-identical guard): SHA-256 hash lookup — catches cosine=1.00, zero cost
- Tier 1 (near-identical guard, write-time): SimHash k=3 — catches cosine >= 0.994 (minor edits, punctuation changes, hook re-fires with timestamp added). 80%+ detection.
- Tier 2 (strong near-duplicate, write-time): SimHash k=8 — catches cosine >= 0.949 (paraphrase with same key terms). This is the tier that Q141's k=3 misses entirely.
- Tier 3 (moderate near-duplicate, compaction pass): SimHash k=11 OR vector cosine >= 0.90 — for the human-review compaction queue, not write-time rejection.

The write-time path runs k=3 (miss triggers k=8 check, or scan for Hamming <= 8 directly). The compaction pass runs k=11. The FPR at k=8 for unrelated content is 4.50e-13 (expected 0.11 pairs at 22K corpus — negligible). At k=11, FPR = 8.89e-10 (expected 222 pairs at 22K — worth monitoring but not a reliability problem).

This is novel because no production AI memory system tunes SimHash k by desired similarity tier. Google uses a fixed k=3 calibrated on their specific workload. Recall's workload (short memories, write-triggered by session hooks, with a defined 3-tier near-duplicate definition from Q121) calls for different k values per tier. The feasibility is high: the SQL scan runs Hamming(fp XOR incoming) <= k with a single k value, and can simply use k=8 as the single write-time guard (which subsumes k=3). Implementation is one integer constant change from Q141's proposed schema.

- **idea-slug**: simhash-k8-write-guard (N=0.72, E=0.82, F=0.85) — Replace k=3 with k=8 as the write-time SimHash guard. This catches cosine >= 0.949 near-duplicates at 80%+ detection rate vs. k=3's cosine >= 0.994 requirement. FPR at k=8 = 4.50e-13 (0.11 expected FP pairs at 22K corpus — zero practical impact). Implementation: change `<= 3` to `<= 8` in the PostgreSQL bit_count comparison. One-line change. The FPR increase from k=3 to k=8 is 4.50e-13 / 2.37e-15 = 190x in per-pair probability, but both are so small that the expected FP count is still ~0 at 22K scale. Detection coverage expands from "only byte-identical with minor noise" to "genuine paraphrases with same key terms."

- **idea-slug**: simhash-content-length-gate (N=0.65, E=0.75, F=0.90) — For memories under 75 characters (< 15 unique tokens), skip SimHash and use exact-match normalization variants (lowercase + strip + collapse whitespace + remove trailing punctuation). Above 75 chars, use SimHash k=8. The content length gate eliminates the SNR instability zone without discarding short memories from deduplication. The implementation is a single `if len(content) < 75` branch before SimHash computation in the write path. No new dependencies.
