# Finding: Q030 — Absence: Multi-Layer Cache with Differentiated Eviction Policies

**Question**: Has any production AI memory system implemented a unified multi-layer cache with distinct eviction policies per layer (e.g., recency-weighted hot cache + quality-based cold store)?
**Agent**: quantitative-analyst
**Question Type**: ABSENCE
**Verdict**: PROMISING
**Severity**: Medium

---

## Mechanism Under Investigation

A production AI memory system with 2+ storage tiers where each tier uses a different *automatic* eviction policy matched to that tier's role — for example, recency-weighted (LRU/ARC) for a hot cache and quality/importance-weighted (utility scoring, heat score) for a cold store — as opposed to (a) a single tier with one uniform policy, or (b) multiple tiers whose transitions are managed by the LLM itself rather than automatic policy logic.

## Evidence

### MemGPT / Letta
MemGPT has two tiers — main context (RAM analog) and external context (archival + recall). The main context eviction is automatic: when the token count exceeds ~70% of the window, older messages are FIFO-evicted after recursive summarization. However, the archival and recall stores have *no automatic eviction*. They are unbounded stores accessed via LLM tool calls. The LLM decides what to archive and when to search. This is LLM-driven management, not policy-based differentiation. Criteria check: FAILS on "different automatic eviction policies at each tier."

### MemoryOS (BAI-LAB, EMNLP 2025 Oral) — CLOSEST MATCH
This is the system that comes closest to the target mechanism. It implements three tiers with genuinely different automatic eviction policies:
- **Short-Term Memory (STM)**: FIFO ordering. When capacity is reached, the oldest dialogue page is transferred to MTM. Pure recency — no quality signal.
- **Mid-Term Memory (MTM)**: Heat-score eviction. Heat = α·N_visit + β·L_interaction + γ·R_recency. When capacity is exceeded, lowest-heat segments are evicted. This is a composite of recency AND frequency AND interaction depth — functionally similar to ARC but with an additional quality proxy (interaction length).
- **Long-Term Personal Memory (LPM)**: Threshold-based promotion only. Segments with heat > τ are promoted; no eviction from LPM (it is the terminal store).

**Assessment**: This *does* implement differentiated per-tier eviction policies. STM uses pure recency (FIFO), MTM uses a composite heat score that blends recency with frequency and quality proxies. The policies are different and automatic.

**However, two gaps remain versus the target design**:
1. The cold store (LPM) has no eviction policy at all — it is effectively unbounded. The Q002/Q003 synthesis calls for a *bounded* cold store with competitive pool displacement.
2. The heat score formula conflates recency and quality into one composite rather than using recency-pure at the hot tier and quality-pure at the cold tier. The policies differ in *degree* more than in *kind*.

**Deployment status**: Research prototype with PyPI package and MCP server. Published as EMNLP 2025 Oral. Not deployed at production scale commercially.

### mem0
Single vector store with optional LRU or decay applied uniformly. No tier differentiation. The PostgreSQL layer stores history as a log, not as a cache with eviction. FAILS all three criteria.

### Zep / Graphiti
Three subgraph layers (episode, semantic entity, community) but no automatic eviction policy at any layer. Graph edges carry temporal validity intervals that are updated on conflict, but outdated edges are invalidated, not evicted by policy. FAILS criteria 1 (no automatic eviction).

### AWS AgentCore Memory
Two tiers: session events (short-term, TTL up to 365 days) and long-term insights (extracted facts). TTL on short-term is time-based, not recency or quality weighted. Long-term has no documented eviction. FAILS criteria 3 (policies not differentiated by tier purpose).

### Cognee
Redis used as transient staging during pipeline execution; once data is promoted to the knowledge graph, the Redis copy is evicted. This is a single promotion event, not an ongoing eviction policy. FAILS criteria 2 (no ongoing differentiated automatic policy).

### MIRIX
Six LLM-agent managers handle six memory types. Core Memory triggers a rewrite at 90% capacity but this is LLM-executed, not automatic policy logic. Other tiers have no specified eviction. FAILS criteria 2 (LLM-driven, not automatic policy).

## Absence Assessment

**Confirmed absent from**: mem0, Zep/Graphiti, AWS AgentCore, Microsoft Semantic Kernel, LangMem, Cognee, MIRIX, LangChain core.

**Partially implemented in**: MemoryOS (BAI-LAB) — genuinely different automatic policies at STM (FIFO/recency) and MTM (heat-score composite), but cold store has no eviction, and the system is a research prototype rather than a commercial production deployment. MemGPT/Letta has automatic eviction only at the hot-tier level (FIFO with summarization); cold store is entirely LLM-managed.

**Classification**: PARTIAL ABSENCE

MemoryOS represents the first published AI memory system to implement automatic, per-tier differentiated eviction policies. It validates the architectural hypothesis that different tiers warrant different policies. However:
- The design is not "recency-pure hot + quality-pure cold" — it is "FIFO hot + composite (recency+frequency+quality) mid + unbounded long."
- The cold store boundary (LPM is unbounded) and the blending of signals in the mid tier leave significant design space unoccupied.
- No commercial production deployment at scale has implemented this pattern.

---

## Why Does This Gap Exist?

1. **Framework authors optimize for recall accuracy, not cache efficiency.** Most memory systems are evaluated on benchmark recall (LoCoMo, LOCOMO F1) rather than on memory efficiency under capacity constraints. Without a capacity-constrained benchmark, differentiated eviction policies have no reward signal.

2. **LLM-as-manager is the path of least resistance.** Delegating memory management to the LLM avoids designing eviction logic. It is flexible but expensive and opaque. Systems like MemGPT choose this path because it requires no manual policy specification.

3. **The database buffer pool analogy has not been adopted.** PostgreSQL shared_buffers (recency-weighted LRU for hot pages) + OS page cache (frequency-weighted for cold pages) is the canonical two-tier differentiated eviction design. No AI memory system has explicitly imported this pattern.

4. **MemoryOS gets closest but is personalization-focused, not capacity-focused.** The heat score in MTM is designed to identify which memories are personally significant to the user — not which are most likely to be queried next. This distinction matters: a quality-based cold store for Recall should optimize retrieval utility, not personal significance.

## Ideas Extracted (gap confirmed)

### Idea: policy-differentiated-tier-cache
- **Novelty**: 0.82 — MemoryOS partially validates the architecture; the specific combination of ARC for hot + competitive-pool-displacement for cold has not been implemented anywhere.
- **Evidence**: 0.78 — MemoryOS demonstrates that differentiated per-tier policies improve benchmark performance (48% F1 improvement). The source mechanisms (ARC, competitive pool) are well-validated in systems caching literature. MemGPT's LLM-driven approach confirms the problem exists.
- **Feasibility**: 0.80 — MemoryOS exists and ships a PyPI package and MCP server. The incremental step is (a) replacing FIFO with ARC at the hot tier, (b) implementing competitive pool displacement at the cold tier, (c) making the cold store bounded. All three are well-understood algorithms.
- **Description**: Implement a three-tier memory cache for Recall with genuinely differentiated eviction policies: (1) Hot tier uses ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache) that balances recency and frequency — the finding from Q001. This tier uses fast Redis or in-memory storage, bounded at ~100 memories. (2) Warm tier uses SWR (stale-while-revalidate) consolidation policy from Q002 — memories here are consolidated and refreshed, not evicted on recency alone. Bounded at ~500 memories. (3) Cold tier uses competitive pool displacement from Q003 — new memories must displace existing ones based on quality score (factual density, semantic uniqueness). Bounded at ~5000 memories. Each tier uses a *different* eviction signal: recency+frequency at hot, consolidation-value at warm, quality at cold. Automatic eviction fires when tier capacity is exceeded, not LLM-driven.

### Idea: heat-score-tiered-improvement
- **Novelty**: 0.55 — MemoryOS's heat score already blends recency and frequency; the improvement is separating these into distinct signals per tier rather than compositing them.
- **Evidence**: 0.85 — MemoryOS empirically validates the three-tier approach. The weakness of composite signals is well-known from ARC literature (ARC was invented precisely because LRU and LFU individually are insufficient).
- **Feasibility**: 0.90 — Direct extension of MemoryOS's existing architecture; replace the composite heat score with separate per-tier policies.
- **Description**: Fork MemoryOS's architecture. At STM: replace FIFO with ARC (adds frequency tracking to pure recency). At MTM: split heat score into separate recency signal and quality signal; use quality signal alone for eviction decisions (not the composite). At LPM: add a bounded competitive pool with quality-based admission threshold. This preserves MemoryOS's three-tier structure while sharpening the policy differentiation between tiers.
