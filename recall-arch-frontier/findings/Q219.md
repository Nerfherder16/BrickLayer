# Finding: Q219 — Memory Hallucination Liability in Regulated Domains

**Question**: How does the legal/compliance community approach liability when an AI system's retrieved memory leads to an incorrect action in a regulated domain? What auditability standards are emerging (FDA, SEC, HIPAA) for AI memory systems? What does "zero hallucination on factual memory" require technically?
**Question Type**: [ADJACENT]
**Verdict**: BREAKTHROUGH
**Severity**: High
**Source field**: Regulatory survey (FDA SaMD guidance Jan 2025 draft, FINRA Notice 24-09, HIPAA 45 C.F.R. § 164.312(b), EU AI Act Articles 12-17, FINOS AI Governance Framework v2 Oct 2025); production audit (Mem0, LangChain, OpenAI Memory, LlamaIndex); ISACA Agentic AI Auditing (2025); liability analysis (Morgan Lewis, TechAndMediaLaw, PMC).

## Regulatory Landscape by Vertical

### FDA (Medical AI / SaMD)

**Status**: Active framework, January 2025 draft guidance in comment period (not yet final).

Key governing documents:
- Good Machine Learning Practice (GMLP) Guiding Principles — Oct 2021 (Final)
- Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) — Oct 2023 / Dec 2024 (Final)
- Transparency for ML-Enabled Medical Devices — Jun 2024 (Final)
- AI-Enabled Device Software Functions: Lifecycle Management — Jan 6, 2025 (Draft)

**What is required**: Documentation of algorithm design, training data, and decision-making logic. Transparency disclosures covering performance metrics, risk management, and data lineage. Ongoing monitoring with documented risk management throughout the lifecycle.

**What is NOT yet specified**: The FDA has not issued specific regulation on hallucination thresholds, RAG/memory retrieval logging, or retrieved-context audit trails. Hallucination disclosure remains a recommendation (model cards) rather than a rule. The January 2025 draft lifecycle guidance is the closest to a comprehensive standard — it is still in comment phase.

---

### SEC / FINRA (Financial AI)

**Status**: No AI-specific regulations. Existing technology-neutral frameworks apply.

Key instruments:
- **FINRA Regulatory Notice 24-09** (June 2024): Existing Rules 3110 (supervision) and 2210 (communications) apply to AI-generated content. Member firms must maintain Gen AI inventories and governance policies.
- **FINRA 2026 Regulatory Oversight Report**: Emphasizes Gen AI governance at both enterprise and individual-associated-person level.
- **SEC Rules 17a-3 and 17a-4**: Comprehensive records required for all business communications and decision-making processes. AI-generated investment recommendations must document input data, decision logic, and human oversight. WORM (Write Once Read Many) storage required for retention.
- **FINOS AI Governance Framework v2** (Oct 2025): Explicitly states *"There is currently no reliable method for removing hallucinations."* Requires vector store access audit logging, foundation model versioning for decision explanation, and hallucination risk management.

**Liability gap**: No court has ruled specifically on liability when a RAG system retrieves incorrect context leading to a bad trade or advice. Existing fiduciary duty standards apply; the institutional firm retains liability — AI creates no new liability shield.

---

### HIPAA / HL7 (Healthcare PHI)

**Governing rule**: HIPAA Security Rule, 45 C.F.R. § 164.312(b) — Audit Controls (written 2003, not amended for AI).

Required audit log fields:
- User identification
- Date and time of access
- Type of action performed (create, read, edit, delete)
- Details of accessed/modified ePHI
- Network access records

**Retention**: Minimum 6 years.

**AI-specific gap**: The 2003 standard contains no requirements for: which retrieved memory entries were used, confidence scores, source attestation for RAG, or retrieval query logging. OCR's December 2024 NPRM tightens enforcement of existing rules but does not address AI-specific logging. This is an unregulated gap that HHS has not yet addressed through rulemaking.

---

### EU AI Act (Cross-sector)

**Regulation**: EU 2024/1689, in force August 1, 2024.
**Full applicability for high-risk AI systems**: **August 2, 2026** — 5 months from the time of this research.

**Article 12 — Automatic Logging Requirements for High-Risk Systems:**
Systems must "technically allow for the automatic recording of events (logs) over the lifetime of the system." For biometric identification (and by interpretive extension, high-risk AI using retrieved context):
- Usage period: start/end timestamps for each use
- **Reference database queried** — directly applicable to memory systems
- Input data that produced results
- Identity of personnel who verified results
- Post-market monitoring events

**Articles 8–15 (collective requirements)**: Risk management lifecycle documentation, data governance measures, technical documentation, human oversight safeguards, accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity requirements, continuous monitoring and incident reporting, capability/limitation disclosure to deployers.

## Liability Attribution (Current State)

**United States (Medical)**: Treating physicians and hospitals bear primary liability under traditional malpractice standards. AI vendors disclaim responsibility contractually. Emerging "reckless disregard" theory under FCA when AI-enabled workflows generate inaccurate billing/diagnostic data despite known red flags.

**United States (Financial)**: Existing fiduciary duty standards apply. Firms that fail to cure known model vulnerabilities and lack corrective policies breach duty of care. AI does not create a new liability shield.

**European Union**: Bifurcated: providers (developers) liable for system conformity as designed; deployers liable for appropriate use, human oversight, and monitoring. EU AI Liability Directive (proposed, not yet adopted) would create presumption of causation when AI Act violations cause harm.

**Core insight**: Liability is currently attributed to humans (physicians, advisers, firms), not to AI systems. The audit trail requirement exists because **if an AI system's retrieved context contributed to a harmful decision, the affected party must be able to prove what was retrieved, from what source, at what confidence level**. Without this trail, causal attribution is impossible.

## Regulatory-Grade Retrieval Audit: Five Required Fields

Synthesizing from FDA GMLP, FINRA Rule 17a-4, HIPAA 45 C.F.R. § 164.312(b), EU AI Act Articles 12–17, and FINOS AI Governance Framework v2:

| Field | Regulatory Basis | Technical Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Retrieval decision lineage** | FINRA 17a-4, EU Act Art.12, HIPAA action details | Log each retrieved chunk ID, similarity score, ranking order, and final selection for each inference event |
| **2. Source attestation** | FDA Transparency/GMLP, FINOS versioning, EU Art.13 | Each retrieved chunk traceable to: origin document, ingestion timestamp, version ID, authoring system |
| **3. Confidence scores persisted** | FDA GMLP Principle 10, EU accuracy safeguards | Vector similarity score and any re-ranking scores at retrieval time, captured and stored (not just exposed at runtime) |
| **4. Human review identity + timestamp** | FDA CDS oversight, EU Art.14, FINRA Rule 3110 | For high-risk outputs: reviewer identity and timestamp logged; retrieved sources visible to reviewer |
| **5. Intent capture** | ISACA agentic AI, FINOS action source tracking | Triggering query or agent goal logged: what caused this retrieval (UserPromptSubmit query hash, PostToolUse event, agent ID) |

**All five fields are absent from all production AI memory systems** (Mem0, LangChain, OpenAI Memory, LlamaIndex). The closest production approach requires a custom observability layer (LangSmith, Arize Phoenix, Galileo) writing to append-only WORM-compliant storage — not provided out of the box by any memory library.

## "Zero Hallucination" — Operational Technical Definition

"Zero hallucination on factual memory" in regulated domains is **not a claim about accuracy** — it is a **governance posture** about groundedness:

### What it technically requires:

1. **Source-grounded outputs**: Every factual claim traceable to a retrieved source document. Ungrounded parametric generation (model inventing facts not in the retrieved context) explicitly blocked for regulated queries. This is the operational definition.

2. **Confidence threshold with fallback**: When retrieval similarity falls below a defined threshold, the system returns "no information found" or escalates to human review — it does NOT generate an answer. The threshold must be documented and justified.

3. **Source currency verification**: Retrieved memories must carry ingestion timestamps. Outputs derived from sources exceeding a maximum staleness threshold (e.g., 18 months for clinical guidelines) must be flagged or blocked.

4. **Human review gates for above-risk-threshold outputs**: High-risk outputs must surface retrieved sources to a qualified reviewer before the output is acted upon. The reviewer's decision and identity must be logged.

5. **Adversarial input monitoring**: Write-time filtering (Q215-Q217 defense stack) to prevent poisoned memories from entering the retrieval corpus — because a factually grounded output from a poisoned memory is still a hallucination by regulatory standards (the source was fabricated).

### What it does NOT mean:

- 100% accuracy — "zero hallucination" means groundedness, not correctness; a retrieved source can itself be wrong
- Achievable through RAG alone — FINOS framework (Oct 2025) explicitly acknowledges "there is currently no reliable method for removing hallucinations"
- Binary confidence thresholds alone — these must be combined with source attestation, currency checks, and human review

## Implications for Recall 2.0 (Factual Memory Type)

From Q218's write audit trail design (unified quarantine + disposition log) and the Q215-Q217 defense stack, Recall 2.0 is already partially positioned for regulated domain compliance:

**Already designed:**
- Write provenance by hook type (Q200 SourceTrust)
- Quarantine queue with disposition tracking (Q215/Q216/Q217)
- Content hash at write time (write audit)
- Source trust tier (UserVerified=0.90 maps to "reviewed by user")

**Gaps for full regulatory compliance:**
- **Retrieval event logging**: each UserPromptSubmit injection event must log: query hash, K retrieved chunk IDs, similarity scores, final ordering, timestamp
- **Source attestation chain**: each memory entry needs: ingestion timestamp, triggering hook event, originating session hash
- **Confidence threshold config**: documented threshold (e.g., cosine >0.72 for retrieval) with fallback behavior spec
- **Human review gate**: UserVerified SourceTrust should require explicit user confirmation interaction before assignment (not just observation)
- **Retention period config**: 6-year retention for HIPAA-regulated deployments; WORM-compatible export for FINRA

**EU AI Act August 2026 deadline**: Article 12 automatic logging is the immediate near-term compliance driver. Recall 2.0 Phase 4 write audit trail (Q218) addresses write-side logging. Retrieval-side event logging is the remaining gap.

## Analytical Summary

**Regulatory liability for AI memory hallucination is currently attributed to deploying humans, not AI systems** — because no AI system provides the audit trail needed to shift liability. The absence of regulatory-grade audit trails in all production memory systems creates a risk for enterprise customers: if they deploy an AI memory system in a regulated context without a custom observability layer, they cannot satisfy their existing regulatory obligations.

**"Zero hallucination" is groundedness governance, not accuracy.** Regulatory frameworks converge on: source-grounded outputs, confidence fallbacks, source currency verification, and human review gates. These are technically achievable in Recall 2.0 Phase 4.

**The EU AI Act August 2, 2026 deadline is the near-term driver.** Article 12 retrieval-side event logging is a specific, implementable requirement with a concrete compliance date. Recall 2.0's existing write audit trail design (Q218) covers write-side. Retrieval-side is the remaining gap — one LMDB append per UserPromptSubmit hook invocation.
