## Evidence

### Mechanism 1: Sharp-Wave Ripples (SWRs) — the offline replay gate

**Origin**: Wilson & McNaughton (1994), "Reactivation of hippocampal ensemble memories during sleep," Science 265:676-679. Mechanism formalized in Buzsáki (1989), "Two-stage model of memory trace formation," Neuroscience 31:551-570. Modern quantification: Girardeau et al. (2009), "Selective suppression of hippocampal ripples impairs spatial memory," Nature Neuroscience 12:1222-1223.

**Precise description — input/output/state/update rule:**

A sharp-wave ripple is a 100-250ms event that occurs in the CA1 layer of the hippocampus during slow-wave sleep (SWS) and quiet wakefulness. The event has two components: (1) a sharp wave — a large-amplitude deflection driven by a burst of Schaffer collateral inputs from CA3 to CA1; (2) a ripple — a 80-200Hz oscillation superimposed on the sharp wave, generated by reciprocal excitatory-inhibitory interactions in CA1 interneuron networks.

**Input**: Population burst in CA3, triggered by spontaneous replay of previously encoded sequences. CA3 is a recurrent network (dense CA3→CA3 collateral connections) — it can spontaneously regenerate activity patterns that were previously driven by experience. The trigger for a CA3 burst is a reduction in cholinergic tone (acetylcholine falls during slow-wave sleep), which releases the CA3 recurrent network from suppression and allows it to fire.

**State maintained**: The CA3 recurrent network maintains attractor states — weight configurations (synaptic weights strengthened during encoding) that bias the network toward reactivating specific ensemble patterns. This is the "what was encoded" state. The magnitude of encoding is determined by the strength of the original experience (see Mechanism 2 below).

**Output**: During each SWR, a compressed (5-10x faster than real-time) replay of the ensemble firing sequence plays out in CA1, timed to the ripple phase. This co-activation of CA1 pyramidal cells drives activation in deep entorhinal cortex (layer V/VI), which provides input to neocortex via the perforant path.

**Selectivity — why this is not random replay:**

Three convergent lines of evidence show SWR replay is selective, not random:

1. **Content selectivity**: Replay preferentially reactivates the trajectories that ended at a reward location (Foster & Wilson 2006, Nature 440:680-683). Cells coding reward-site-adjacent positions had higher replay probability than cells coding far-from-reward positions. This was demonstrated in rats running linear tracks — the replay order matched the experience order, and the terminal (reward-adjacent) portion was overrepresented.

2. **Novel > familiar selectivity**: Karlsson & Frank (2009), "Awake replay of remote experiences in the hippocampus," Nature Neuroscience 12:913-918. Novel environments produced significantly more replay events per unit time than familiar environments. Novelty — operationally defined as first exposure to an environment — increased SWR rate approximately 2-3x compared to familiar environments.

3. **Prediction-error selectivity**: Ambrose et al. (2016), "Reverse replay of hippocampal place cells is uniquely modulated by changing reward," Neuron 91:1124-1136. When reward location was changed (prediction error condition), both forward and reverse replay of the new route were elevated vs. when reward location was unchanged. Specifically, reverse replay (CA1 firing sequence runs backwards from goal to start) was uniquely elevated after reward change — proposed mechanism: reverse replay enables credit assignment backward through the trajectory.

**Girardeau et al. (2009) — causal evidence**: Delivered electrical pulses timed to disrupt SWRs during post-task sleep in rats. Result: spatial memory on a maze task was selectively impaired vs. controls. This is the strongest evidence that SWR replay is causally necessary for consolidation, not merely correlated with it.

**Update rule for whether a memory gets replayed:**

The probability of an ensemble being replayed during a SWR scales with:
- Synaptic weight magnitude in CA3 collaterals (set by NMDA-receptor-dependent LTP during encoding)
- Novelty of the experience (via dopaminergic modulation — see Mechanism 2)
- Association with reward or salient outcome (via dopaminergic input from VTA)
- Recency (more recently encoded ensembles have higher initial synaptic weights before decay)

Ensembles with weak encoding (low LTP), low novelty, and no reward association have low replay probability during SWRs — they fade without transfer. This is the selection mechanism.

**Failure modes:**
- Retrograde interference: a strong new experience can prevent replay of a weaker earlier experience by dominating the CA3 attractor space during the same sleep period
- Sleep deprivation eliminates SWR replay → no consolidation (well-replicated in rodents and humans)
- The mechanism is lossy — not all encoded patterns replay even once; those that don't replay during the critical window are not consolidated

### Mechanism 2: Prediction Error / Novelty Gating — the encoding filter that determines what enters the consolidation pipeline

**Origin**: McClelland, McNaughton, O'Reilly (1995), "Why there are complementary learning systems in the hippocampus and neocortex: insights from the successes and failures of connectionist models of learning and memory," Psychological Review 102:419-457. This is the Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) paper. Dopaminergic mechanism: Lisman & Grace (2005), "The hippocampal-VTA loop: controlling the entry of information into long-term memory," Neuron 46:703-713.

**The CLS framework — what it predicts:**

CLS distinguishes two learning systems:
1. **Hippocampus**: fast, episodic, high-capacity, separated. Learns a specific event in one trial. Prone to catastrophic interference if asked to support slow statistical learning.
2. **Neocortex**: slow, statistical, overlapping representations. Integrates over many experiences. Cannot learn a specific event quickly without destroying existing knowledge.

The prediction: the hippocampus should selectively encode experiences that violate predictions made by the neocortex — i.e., experiences that carry new information. Experiences the neocortex already predicts well carry no new information and don't need hippocampal storage.

**The dopaminergic gate — Lisman & Grace (2005):**

Input: novelty/prediction-error signal generated by comparing hippocampal output (current experience) against neocortical prediction (prior knowledge). This comparison happens at the subiculum/entorhinal cortex interface.

State: baseline dopamine (DA) tone at CA1/CA3 synapses (set by tonic firing of VTA dopamine neurons).

Update rule:
- If experience matches neocortical prediction (familiar): hippocampal output to subiculum is low. Subiculum drives low output to nucleus accumbens (NAcc). NAcc inhibits ventral pallidum. Ventral pallidum fails to disinhibit VTA. VTA maintains baseline DA tone. Hippocampal encoding remains at baseline (weak LTP).
- If experience violates neocortical prediction (novel): hippocampal output to subiculum is high. Subiculum drives high output to NAcc. NAcc activates ventral pallidum. Ventral pallidum disinhibits VTA. VTA fires a burst. VTA axons release dopamine onto hippocampal CA1 synapses. Dopamine activates D1/D5 receptors, which lower the threshold for NMDA-receptor-dependent LTP via PKA/CREB phosphorylation. Encoding is strengthened — the memory is written with higher synaptic weights.

**Output**: memories with high novelty/prediction-error are encoded with stronger synaptic weights in CA3 → higher replay probability during subsequent SWRs → higher probability of consolidation to neocortex.

**Quantification**: Shohamy & Wagner (2008), "Integrating memories in the human brain: hippocampal-midbrain encoding of overlapping events," Neuron 60:378-389. fMRI showing VTA and hippocampus both respond to prediction-error-inducing stimuli. Wittmann et al. (2005), "Reward-related fMRI activation of dopaminergic midbrain is associated with enhanced hippocampus-dependent long-term memory formation," Neuron 45:459-467: prediction-error-correlated activation predicted subsequent memory performance (2-day retention) with r=0.72.

**The specific novelty computation:**

Leutgeb et al. (2007), "Pattern separation in the dentate gyrus and CA3 of the hippocampus," Science 315:961-966. Novelty at the input level is computed by the dentate gyrus via pattern separation (Mechanism 4). The DG outputs a sparse, orthogonalized representation. The CA3 recurrent network performs pattern completion on this input. If the CA3 pattern-completed representation matches a stored attractor strongly, the experience is classified as familiar. If it matches weakly or generates a new pattern, it is classified as novel. The mismatch between pattern completion output and DG input is the novelty signal.

### Mechanism 3: Systems Consolidation — what transfers from hippocampus to neocortex and the selectivity of transfer

**Origin**: McClelland et al. (1995) CLS theory. Empirical support: Frankland & Bontempi (2005), "The organization of recent and remote memories," Nature Reviews Neuroscience 6:119-130. Mechanistic update: Lesburgueres et al. (2011), "Early tagging of cortical networks is required for the formation of enduring associative memory," Science 331:924-928.

**The two-stage model (precise description):**

Stage 1 (online encoding, minutes to hours): An experience activates hippocampal ensembles via Schaffer collateral circuits. NMDA-receptor-dependent LTP strengthens CA3→CA1 and entorhinal→CA3 synapses. The memory is now retrievable via hippocampal indexing (the hippocampus stores a compressed pointer that can reactivate the neocortical pattern). But the neocortical representation remains weakly encoded — it requires repeated hippocampal reactivation to consolidate.

Stage 2 (offline consolidation, days to weeks): During SWR replay, the hippocampus repeatedly reactivates its CA1 population. This activity propagates to deep entorhinal cortex → neocortex (prefrontal, cingulate, association areas). Each reactivation drives Hebbian potentiation of neocortical synapses encoding the experience. Over many replay events, the neocortical representation accumulates enough synaptic weight to be retrievable without hippocampal indexing.

**What transfers (selectivity rule):**

The neocortical representation is statistical — it integrates the schema-consistent elements of the experience across many replay events. Schema-consistent elements (parts that match existing neocortical knowledge structure) are strengthened faster by replay because they activate existing neocortical attractors. Schema-inconsistent elements (truly novel specifics) require more replay events to consolidate and may not consolidate if replay probability is low.

Preston & Eichenbaum (2013), "Interplay of hippocampus and prefrontal cortex in memory," Current Biology 23:R764-R773: PFC is activated during consolidation and appears to direct which memories consolidate by modulating SWR occurrence during sleep — PFC-hippocampus dialogue during slow-wave sleep. Recent memories that are "goal-relevant" (based on PFC value signal) have elevated ripple rates.

**The transfer is NOT a copy**: The neocortical representation after consolidation is a schema-consistent abstraction, not the episodic original. Specific details (time, place, sensory context) may not consolidate, while the semantic content (what happened, causal relationships, value associations) consolidates more reliably.

**Failure modes**: Schema-inconsistent content has lower transfer probability. Emotionally neutral, low-reward memories consolidate less reliably than emotionally salient or rewarded memories. Memories that are never replayed (suppressed by stronger competing memories) may fail to consolidate entirely.

### Mechanism 4: Pattern Separation vs. Pattern Completion — dentate gyrus gating that determines "new memory vs. update existing memory"

**Origin**: Marr (1971), "Simple memory: A theory for archicortex," Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B 262:23-81. Empirical validation: Leutgeb et al. (2007); O'Reilly & McClelland (1994), "Hippocampal conjunctive encoding, storage, and recall: avoiding a tradeoff," Hippocampus 4:661-682.

**Pattern separation — dentate gyrus (DG):**

Input: Entorhinal cortex (EC) layer II sends the current pattern of activity to DG granule cells via the perforant path. This input represents the current experience as a high-dimensional vector over EC neurons (roughly 200,000 neurons in rat).

State: DG granule cells (~1,000,000 in rat; ~10:1 expansion from EC) are very sparsely active (< 1% active at any time, compared to ~5-10% in EC). This expansion + sparsification is implemented by strong inhibitory interneuron networks (basket cells, axo-axonic cells) that enforce competitive inhibition (winner-take-all).

Update rule: each DG granule cell integrates inputs from EC via perforant path synapses with random connection weights (the DG does not learn — its connections are fixed random projections, implementing a locality-sensitive hash). A small subset of cells with the strongest input activation fire; inhibition silences the rest.

Output: a sparse binary vector (~10,000 active cells out of 1,000,000) that is maximally orthogonal to vectors produced by similar but distinct inputs. Two inputs that share 90% overlap in EC will produce overlapping DG outputs sharing approximately 5-10% of active cells. This is pattern separation — it creates discriminable codes for similar experiences.

**Pattern completion — CA3:**

Input: DG output (via mossy fiber projections, which are strong "detonator" synapses) + direct EC input (via perforant path).

State: CA3 is a recurrent network. Associative LTP during encoding strengthens CA3→CA3 collateral synapses that connect co-active cells. These collateral connections implement a Hopfield-network-like attractor memory.

Update rule: given a partial or degraded version of a previously encoded pattern (from DG or direct EC input), CA3 recurrent dynamics converge to the nearest stored attractor. The network fills in missing pattern components.

Output: a complete pattern over CA3 cells, representing the best-matching stored memory.

**The gating decision — how DG output determines store vs. retrieve:**

If the current EC input is sufficiently similar to a stored CA3 pattern, the DG output will be very different from the DG output that was active during the original encoding (because DG pattern-separates even similar inputs). When this highly separated DG output enters CA3 via mossy fibers, it fails to trigger the expected attractor — the CA3 recurrent dynamics settle into a different or new pattern. This mismatch signal reaches CA1 and is forwarded to the subiculum, where it generates the novelty/prediction-error signal described in Mechanism 2. New storage is triggered.

If the current EC input is identical or very close to a stored CA3 pattern, the DG output shares more active cells with the original encoding's DG output. The CA3 attractor fires strongly (pattern completion wins). This generates a match signal — the experience is familiar. No new storage is triggered; instead, the existing memory's synaptic weights are refreshed (retrieval-induced reconsolidation).

**Leutgeb et al. (2007) — quantification**: Rats explored environments with incrementally modified shapes (from circle to square). DG cells showed "remapping" (complete change of which cells were active) even for small environmental changes (15-20% modification). CA3 cells showed gradual "morphing" between stored patterns, consistent with pattern completion. This confirms the distinct roles: DG separates, CA3 completes.

**The threshold**: There is a parametric similarity threshold. Below threshold (inputs are sufficiently different): DG separation dominates, new memory is encoded. Above threshold (inputs are sufficiently similar): CA3 completion dominates, existing memory is updated/retrieved. The threshold is not fixed — it can shift based on neuromodulatory state (acetylcholine level from medial septum). High ACh (alert, novel environment): threshold shifts toward pattern separation (more new encoding). Low ACh (familiar, sleep): threshold shifts toward pattern completion (more retrieval/replay).

Hasselmo (2006), "The role of acetylcholine in learning and memory," Current Opinion in Neurobiology 16:710-715: ACh level is the parameter that shifts the DG/CA3 balance. This is the biological parameter that determines whether the hippocampus operates in "encode new memory" mode or "retrieve existing memory" mode.

### Track record in source field

All four mechanisms are among the best-validated results in systems neuroscience:

**SWR replay**: Wilson & McNaughton (1994) cited >2,700 times. Girardeau et al. (2009) causal disruption experiment replicated by Ego-Stengel & Wilson (2010), Jadhav et al. (2012). SWR disruption reliably impairs spatial and associative memory in rats across multiple labs. Human SWR analogs (hippocampal ripples in iEEG) confirmed by Staresina et al. (2015), Nature Neuroscience 18:1679-1686.

(2009), Science 324:1080-1084. Dopamine in hippocampus enhancing LTP confirmed pharmacologically across dozens of studies.

**Pattern separation**: Leutgeb et al. (2007) cited >900 times. Human DG pattern separation confirmed with high-resolution fMRI: Bakker et al. (2008), Science 319:1640-1642. Directly relevant to encoding selectivity.

### Why AI memory systems don't do this yet

**Reason 1 — the store-everything default**: Every AI memory system treats storage as cheap and retrieval as the problem. The neurological intuition — that what is *not* stored is as important as what is stored — runs counter to how software engineers think about persistence layers. Disk is cheap; why evict at write time?

**Reason 2 — no novelty signal at write time**: The hippocampal mechanism requires comparing an incoming experience against existing knowledge to generate a prediction-error/novelty score *before* deciding storage depth. Recall's current store operation accepts a blob and stores it unconditionally. Generating a novelty signal requires a semantic comparison against the existing vector store — an embedding lookup at write time, which adds latency to the store path and requires a minimum-similarity threshold design decision.

**Reason 3 — no two-stage pipeline**: Consolidation requires separating "recently encoded, not yet stable" memories from "consolidated, stable" memories. Building this requires a buffer (analogous to hippocampus) and a consolidation process that runs offline (analogous to SWR replay during sleep). No AI memory system has an offline consolidation process. MemGPT approximates this with explicit paging but it is driven by context window overflow, not a consolidation algorithm.

**Reason 4 — the ACh parameter is not implemented anywhere**: The acetylcholine-level parameter that shifts the system between "encode new" and "retrieve existing" mode is conceptually the most actionable insight. At the start of a new task (high novelty, need to encode aggressively), the threshold for storing a new vs. updating an existing memory should shift toward separation. During retrieval-heavy operations, the threshold should shift toward completion. No system implements a dynamic encoding threshold that shifts based on detected novelty of the current session.

### Idea: prediction-error-novelty-gate

- **Novelty**: 0.90 — No production AI memory system (MemGPT, mem0, Zep, Recall, LangChain Memory) computes a novelty/prediction-error score at write time and uses it to gate storage. The specific mechanism — compare incoming embedding against top-k nearest neighbors in the existing store, compute a minimum distance to nearest stored memory, and use this distance as a "novelty score" that modulates storage depth — is absent from all known production AI memory systems.
- **Evidence**: 0.85 — McClelland et al. (1995) CLS theory is one of the most cited papers in cognitive neuroscience (4,000+ citations). Lisman & Grace (2005) hippocampal-VTA loop provides the specific algorithmic mechanism. Wittmann et al. (2005) shows prediction error score predicts 2-day retention with r=0.72. The mechanism is validated, not hypothetical.
- **Feasibility**: 0.75 — Recall already has Qdrant and embeds every stored memory. At write time, before storing a new memory, query Qdrant for the top-3 most similar memories. Compute the minimum cosine distance to the nearest neighbor. If distance < threshold (similar content already exists): assign low importance score, skip or merge. If distance > threshold (novel content): assign high importance score, store with full indexing including Neo4j graph edges. The core computation is one Qdrant search (already on the hot path in retrieve; needs to be added to store). The threshold value requires calibration but can start at 0.15 cosine distance (empirically common in retrieval deduplication). Implementation is 1-2 weeks in FastAPI, touching only the store endpoint.
- **Description**: Add a pre-storage novelty check to Recall's store operation. Before persisting any incoming memory, query Qdrant for the top-3 nearest neighbors. Compute the minimum cosine distance to the nearest stored memory. If the nearest neighbor is within 0.15 cosine distance (very similar content already exists), the incoming memory is classified as low-novelty: its importance score is penalized by 50%, it is stored in PostgreSQL/Qdrant but excluded from Neo4j graph indexing (no relationship edges created), and its Redis TTL is shortened to 6 hours. If the nearest neighbor is more than 0.30 cosine distance away (genuinely novel content), the memory is classified as high-novelty: full importance score, full indexing including Neo4j, 48-hour Redis TTL. This directly implements the hippocampal prediction-error gate: novel experiences are encoded deeply (full stack), familiar experiences are encoded shallowly (vector only, no graph, short TTL). The threshold acts as the biological "novelty threshold" parameter.

### Idea: swr-offline-consolidation-batch

- **Novelty**: 0.85 — No production AI memory system runs an offline consolidation process analogous to SWR replay. MemGPT's paging is triggered by context overflow, not by a scheduled consolidation algorithm. The specific idea of a session-boundary consolidation job that: (1) identifies which memories from the last session are worth promoting to long-term storage, (2) merges near-duplicate memories, (3) updates importance scores based on retrieval frequency during the session — is absent from all known production systems.
- **Evidence**: 0.80 — The SWR replay consolidation model (Buzsáki 1989, Wilson & McNaughton 1994, Girardeau et al. 2009) provides the motivation. The specific algorithmic implementation for Recall would be novel engineering, so evidence for the exact implementation is lower than evidence for the biological mechanism. Score reflects the strength of the biological source evidence.
- **Feasibility**: 0.70 — Recall already has a `recall-session-summary.js` hook that fires at session end (Stop event). This is the "sleep onset" analog — the moment when online encoding ends and offline consolidation should begin. The consolidation job needs to: (1) query PostgreSQL for all memories stored in the last session window (last 4 hours, or since the last session-start event); (2) for each memory in the session window, query Qdrant for its top-5 nearest neighbors in the full store; (3) cluster near-duplicates (cosine distance < 0.10) and merge them into a single memory with the highest importance score of the cluster; (4) update importance scores in PostgreSQL based on how often each memory was retrieved during the session (access count already tracked). The pipeline runs as a background FastAPI task triggered by the session-end hook. Expected runtime: 2-10 seconds for a typical session (20-50 stored memories). Implementation is 2-3 weeks.
- **Description**: Add a `POST /consolidate` endpoint to Recall's FastAPI server that runs at session end (triggered by the Stop hook alongside the existing session-summary storage). The consolidation job runs three passes over the session's new memories: (1) deduplication pass — cluster memories with cosine distance < 0.10, keep highest-importance representative, delete duplicates from Qdrant + PostgreSQL; (2) importance update pass — for each memory retrieved ≥ 2 times during the session, increase importance score by 0.1 (up to max 1.0), simulating the SWR replay strengthening effect; (3) promotion pass — memories with importance score ≥ 0.7 get Neo4j graph edges created (if not already present), making them discoverable via graph traversal. Memories that were stored in the session but never retrieved (importance score < 0.3, retrieval count = 0) get their TTL set to 24 hours with no auto-renewal — they will expire from Redis and eventually be evicted from the Qdrant hot index if a cold-tiering mechanism is implemented.

### Idea: ach-dynamic-encoding-threshold

- **Novelty**: 0.88 — The concept of a dynamic encoding threshold that shifts based on session context (high-novelty session vs. retrieval-heavy session) is absent from all known AI memory systems. The closest concept is MemGPT's manual memory management but it is not automatic, not novelty-driven, and not threshold-based.
- **Evidence**: 0.75 — Hasselmo (2006) provides the ACh mechanism. The principle is well-validated: ACh level reliably shifts hippocampal network state between encoding-dominant and retrieval-dominant modes. However, the direct implementation evidence is in neuroscience rodent/human studies, not AI memory engineering. The translation requires a surrogate for ACh level (session novelty index), which has not been validated in AI memory contexts.
- **Feasibility**: 0.65 — Requires instrumenting session context to produce a "session novelty index" — an estimate of how much new information is being encountered relative to retrieved information. Operationally: compute the ratio of (new memories stored in last 10 minutes) / (memories retrieved in last 10 minutes). High ratio = high-novelty mode (ACh-high analog, shift toward pattern separation). Low ratio = retrieval-heavy mode (ACh-low analog, shift toward pattern completion). The encoding threshold — the minimum novelty score for a new memory to be stored — is then a function of this ratio. When the session novelty index is high (early in a new project), the threshold is low (store aggressively, even somewhat redundant memories). When the index is low (steady-state usage of a familiar project), the threshold is high (only store genuinely novel content). Implementation requires: a sliding window counter on store + retrieve events (trackable in Redis with INCR/EXPIRE), a threshold lookup table mapping novelty index to minimum cosine distance, and a small change to the store endpoint. Complexity is moderate; the main challenge is calibrating the threshold lookup table without empirical data from actual sessions.
- **Description**: Add session-context-aware dynamic encoding to Recall's store operation. Recall maintains a Redis sliding window (last 10 minutes) counting: stores_count (memories stored) and retrieves_count (memories retrieved). The session novelty index = stores_count / max(retrieves_count, 1). At store time, this index determines the minimum novelty threshold: high index (> 2.0: storing more than retrieving) → low threshold (0.05 minimum cosine distance, store aggressively); medium index (0.5-2.0) → medium threshold (0.15 minimum cosine distance, default); low index (< 0.5: retrieving heavily, not much new) → high threshold (0.30 minimum cosine distance, only store genuinely new content). This mirrors the biological ACh mechanism: the system automatically shifts into encoding mode when encountering new material and into retrieval/completion mode during familiar work sessions, without any manual configuration.

### Idea: dg-pattern-separation-dedup

- **Novelty**: 0.80 — Embedding-based deduplication exists (e.g., some RAG pipelines do pre-storage similarity checks). The novel element here is the two-mode architecture: not just "is this a duplicate?" but specifically implementing the DG-CA3 distinction — very similar inputs trigger an update to the existing memory (pattern completion), while sufficiently dissimilar inputs create a new memory (pattern separation). The update-vs-create decision driven by a similarity threshold is a cleaner formulation than "dedup" and produces a different outcome: existing memories are enriched with new details from near-duplicate inputs rather than discarded or redundantly stored.
- **Evidence**: 0.80 — Leutgeb et al. (2007), Bakker et al. (2008) provide strong validation for the DG pattern separation mechanism and the threshold-based store/update decision. The cosine distance threshold to implement this is an engineering choice, but the principle that similar inputs should update rather than create is strongly supported.
- **Feasibility**: 0.80 — At store time, query Qdrant for the top-1 nearest neighbor. If cosine distance < 0.10 (very similar — high overlap, "same memory"): perform an append/merge operation on the existing memory's text content (concatenate the new content to the existing memory, re-embed the merged content, update Qdrant vector). If cosine distance 0.10-0.25 (moderately similar — "related memory, different episode"): store as a new memory AND create a Neo4j edge of type RELATED_TO between the new memory and the existing near-neighbor. If cosine distance > 0.25 (dissimilar — "genuinely new"): store normally. This is a 3-way decision tree at store time. Implementation cost: low (2-3 days), touches only the store endpoint.
- **Description**: Replace Recall's current unconditional store with a three-way store decision. Incoming memory is compared against the nearest stored memory by cosine distance. Distance < 0.10: merge mode — the incoming content enriches an existing memory (the memory's text is updated, its re-embedding replaces the old vector, its importance score is increased by 0.05 to reflect the additional confirmation). Distance 0.10-0.25: extend mode — a new memory is created AND linked to the nearest neighbor via a RELATED_TO edge in Neo4j; the new memory's importance score inherits a fraction (0.3×) of the existing memory's importance score, bootstrapping its relevance. Distance > 0.25: create mode — new memory stored at face-value importance score with no automatic linking. This directly implements the DG-CA3 gate: high-overlap inputs strengthen existing memories (pattern completion), low-overlap inputs create new memories (pattern separation). The outcome is a richer, less redundant memory store where related memories form explicit clusters rather than scattered duplicates.

- **[ABSENCE] Q007 direct test**: The `swr-offline-consolidation-batch` idea assumes no production AI memory system runs an offline consolidation process. This should be explicitly verified: examine MemGPT, mem0, Zep, and LangChain's ConversationSummaryBufferMemory implementations for any scheduled or session-end consolidation job. Falsifiable hypothesis: no production system runs a consolidation job at session end that merges near-duplicate memories AND updates importance scores AND promotes high-importance memories to a graph layer.

- **[PHYSICS] new question**: What is the minimum latency overhead of adding the prediction-error-novelty-gate to Recall's store operation? The gate requires one Qdrant ANN query at store time. Measure: (1) current store latency p95; (2) Qdrant top-3 search latency at current corpus size; (3) compute the store latency budget available (< 100ms is acceptable for a background hook). If Qdrant top-3 search exceeds 50ms at corpus size > 50,000 memories, the novelty gate may need to be made asynchronous. Falsifiable via benchmarking at C:/Users/trg16/Dev/Recall.

- **[ADVERSARIAL] new question**: Is the cosine distance threshold (0.15 for novelty gate, 0.10 for merge) the right metric for the DG pattern-separation function, or is angular distance over the raw embedding space the wrong measure for this purpose? The DG implements locality-sensitive hashing via random projections. Cosine similarity is a dot product in normalized space — it measures angular similarity. But the "same memory vs. different memory" decision may require a different metric (e.g., Jaccard similarity over activated token features, or Mahalanobis distance that accounts for embedding space anisotropy). This should be tested before hardcoding the threshold.

- **[ADJACENT] new question**: Immunological memory (Q003) implements a parallel mechanism: high-affinity B-cells (strong novelty response) produce long-lived plasma cells (long-term storage), while low-affinity responses produce short-lived effector cells (short-term storage). Does affinity maturation in germinal centers provide a different algorithmic handle on the store/consolidate decision than hippocampal prediction error? The immune mechanism operates on a different timescale and may produce different engineering insights than the hippocampal mechanism — particularly around multi-round iterative improvement of stored representations.

- **[TIMESHIFTED] hypothesis**: The `ach-dynamic-encoding-threshold` idea requires calibrating a lookup table mapping session novelty index to cosine distance threshold. In 2026, this lookup table is a hand-tuned heuristic. In 2032, this parameter is likely learned from actual session data — the system observes which stored memories proved useful (retrieval frequency, retrieval-to-action conversion) and uses this signal to train the threshold function. The 2026 version should be designed with this future learning step in mind: the threshold should be a pluggable function, not a hardcoded table.
