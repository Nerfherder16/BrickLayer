# Finding: Q018 — Adversarial: Is Cosine Similarity Correct for AI Memory Retrieval?

**Question**: Is cosine similarity over fixed-dimension embeddings the correct retrieval signal for AI memory, or is it fundamentally wrong for the use case?
**Agent**: quantitative-analyst
**Question Type**: ADVERSARIAL
**Verdict**: BREAKTHROUGH
**Severity**: High

---

## Defender Thread: Cosine Is Correct

### The Strongest Case for Cosine Similarity

**Formal properties that make it right**

Cosine similarity is the unique function that satisfies four axioms simultaneously:

1. **Symmetry**: sim(A, B) = sim(B, A) — meaning-overlap is symmetric
2. **Self-identity is maximal**: sim(A, A) ≥ sim(A, B) for all B
3. **Scale invariance**: sim(λa, b) = sim(a, b) — meaning does not scale with representation magnitude
4. **Smoothness**: continuous, so small perturbations in representation space produce small perturbations in score

Axiom 3 is the load-bearing one. It is derivable from the principle that a text's semantic content should not depend on the norm of its embedding vector — a commitment about what the embedding space is modeling. From these four axioms alone, the inner product of unit-normalized vectors (cosine similarity) is the unique function on R^D that satisfies all of them simultaneously. This is not a design choice — it is a uniqueness result.

**Production validation at scale**

Cosine similarity has been the primary retrieval signal in every production semantic search system of note: Google's Sentence-BERT models, OpenAI's text-embedding-ada-002 (used in millions of RAG pipelines), Facebook's DPR for open-domain QA, every Qdrant/Weaviate/Pinecone deployment. The question of "is cosine the right signal for semantic search?" has been answered empirically and at scale: yes, for the purpose of finding semantically related text from a corpus.

The embedding models — including qwen3-embedding:0.6b — were explicitly trained to make cosine similarity a calibrated proxy for semantic relevance. The training objective (contrastive learning on (query, relevant passage, irrelevant passage) triples) specifically minimizes the cosine distance between semantically related pairs. The model's geometry is organized so that cosine distance is the canonical measure.

**What alternatives are meaningfully worse?**

The practical alternatives are:
- **BM25/TF-IDF**: captures lexical overlap but collapses on paraphrase, synonym, or abbreviation variation. "CasaOS" vs "the home server" retrieves nothing. Cosine handles this naturally.
- **Euclidean distance on raw embeddings**: violates scale invariance — the norm of the embedding vector varies with text length, formality, and model internal quirks. Two texts with the same meaning but different lengths get different norms, making Euclidean distance a measure of (meaning + length + model artifact) rather than meaning alone.
- **Exact match / keyword search**: requires knowing the exact vocabulary used at storage time. Fails completely for natural language queries against conversational memories.
- **BM25 + cosine hybrid**: better than either alone, but cosine is still doing most of the heavy lifting for semantic matching.

**Operating regime where cosine is optimal**

Cosine is optimal when:
1. The corpus contains semantically diverse content (low redundancy)
2. Queries are drawn from the same distribution as stored content (symmetric speech acts)
3. The relevant signal is semantic topic/meaning similarity
4. The embedding model has been trained on the same domain as the stored content
5. Corpus size is small enough that the top-K candidate pool is well-sampled from the full distribution

**Core claim**: Cosine similarity is the formally correct measure of semantic proximity in a unit-hypersphere embedding space, validated at scale across every major production retrieval system.

**Evidence**: Uniqueness proof from the four axioms (Q014 derivation). Production deployment in Sentence-BERT, DPR, OpenAI embeddings. Karpukhin et al. 2020 (DPR) demonstrating cosine inner product as the canonical dense retrieval signal.

**Best case scenario**: Cosine retrieval on a clean, diverse, well-maintained Recall store with high-quality qwen3 embeddings retrieves the top-5 most semantically relevant memories with >80% precision. The importance score multiplier correctly boosts higher-quality memories. The system consistently surfaces the right infrastructure fact when asked about it.

**Falsifying condition for the defender**: The defender's case collapses when the content distribution violates the "semantically diverse" assumption — specifically, when (a) the corpus contains high-redundancy paraphrase clusters that flood the top-K with semantically identical content (Q013 shows this occurs at 92% of queries with R=0.40), (b) queries are structurally different from stored content (different speech acts — questions vs. statements), (c) structurally similar sentences refer to referentially distinct entities (Q026: "redis_host is 192.168.50.19" vs. "redis_host is 192.168.50.62" are cosine-near but referentially disjoint), or (d) the relevant signal is temporal or causal rather than semantic.

## Attacker Thread: Cosine Is Wrong for Memory

### The Strongest Case That Cosine Is Wrong for AI Memory

**Memory is not search — the formal difference**

Search retrieval answers: "Given query Q, find the k text chunks from corpus C most likely to contain the answer."
The corpus is static, curated, and partitioned from the queries. Queries and documents are different objects.

Memory retrieval answers: "Given the current context K, find the k memories from store M most useful for generating a good response."

The difference is not surface-level. In memory:
1. The "query" and the stored content are the same type of object (both are observations about the world made by the same agent)
2. The relevance criterion is not "contains the answer" but "would reduce the agent's uncertainty in a useful way right now"
3. The store is dynamic, personal, and accumulates redundancy
4. Temporal ordering of memory creation is part of the signal (newer override older)
5. Causal relationships between memories are retrievable signals

Cosine similarity compresses all five of these dimensions to one scalar. It can only capture (1) partially (same embedding space for query and memory), and it fails (2), (3), (4), and (5) entirely.

**What cosine structurally cannot represent**

**Temporal signal (Q006)**: Cosine is time-blind. A memory created 2 years ago about "Redis config" is cosine-identical to a memory created yesterday about "Redis config." No temporal decay, no recency signal. Q006 confirms: no production system implements temporal weighting in the ANN traversal itself — all do post-ANN re-ranking, which means the top-100 candidate pool is already biased toward old, high-cosine memories.

**Causal signal (Q010)**: "Why did X happen?" requires traversing a causal chain. Cosine retrieves semantically similar memories, but similarity does not equal causation. "Redis is down" and "the memory eviction policy is set to noeviction" are both semantically related to a Redis outage query — but only the second is causally relevant. Cosine cannot distinguish semantic relatedness from causal relevance. Q010 confirms: graph-walk retrieval (PPR) outperforms vector-only on multi-hop QA by 20% (HippoRAG NeurIPS 2024).

**Redundancy amplification (Q013)**: Cosine returns the K most similar vectors. In a store with 50% redundancy (R=0.50), the top-K is dominated by paraphrase copies of the top-1 result. Q013 proves: with R=0.40, 92% of top-5 retrievals contain at least one paraphrase duplicate, reducing effective K from 5 to 3. Cosine is maximally bad at redundancy-avoidance because near-duplicates are, by construction, cosine-near.

**Referential confusion (Q026)**: Cosine cannot distinguish "semantically similar because same fact" from "semantically similar because same sentence template applied to different referent." "Redis host is 192.168.50.19" and "Redis host is 192.168.50.62" are cosine-near (same predicate structure, same subject, similar object type) but referentially disjoint (two different machines). Q026 identifies this as Recall's actual production failure mode.

**Asymmetric speech acts (Q014)**: Queries are questions; stored memories are statements. These are different speech acts embedded in different geometric regions of the embedding space. A query like "what is the Redis host on CasaOS?" and the stored memory "Redis host on CasaOS is 192.168.50.19" are not geometrically near in symmetric cosine space — the question encodes interrogative structure, the statement encodes declarative structure. DPR (bi-encoder) addresses this with asymmetric projection; pure cosine does not.

**Anisotropy distortion (Q008)**: Cosine on raw embeddings overweights high-variance dimensions. The qwen3-embedding:0.6b space is anisotropic — most of the variance is concentrated in a small subset of dimensions. This means cosine is dominated by those dimensions regardless of their semantic relevance to the specific query. Q013 establishes that intrinsic dimensionality of sentence embedding spaces is 10–50 dims out of 1024, meaning 97.6% of the cosine computation is processing noise.

**What the correct retrieval signal looks like from first principles**

Starting from information theory: a memory is useful if it maximally reduces the agent's uncertainty in the current context. The correct retrieval score is therefore the mutual information I(memory; context) between the memory and the current context. Cosine similarity is an approximation to I(memory; context) in the semantic dimension only. The full signal has four orthogonal components:

```
U(memory, context, time) = sem(m, q)^β        # Semantic similarity (cosine approximation)
                          × decay(age(m))^γ    # Temporal relevance (Q006: power-law decay)
                          × causal(m, graph)^δ # Causal proximity (Q010: PPR)
                          × novelty(m, K)^ε    # Information gain vs already-retrieved set
```

Where the exponents β, γ, δ, ε are learned from user feedback (which retrieved memories led to useful completions). Cosine implements β only, setting γ = δ = ε = 0.

**Evidence that cosine underperforms for memory specifically**

- Q013: 92% of top-5 retrievals contain a paraphrase duplicate (direct evidence of cosine's redundancy failure for memory)
- Q014: queries and stored facts occupy different geometric regions (direct evidence of the asymmetric speech act failure)
- Q026: confirmed production failure where cosine merges infrastructure facts for different machines (direct evidence of referential confusion)
- Q006: no production AI memory system uses temporal decay in the ANN traversal — the absence of this signal means cosine is operating with one hand tied
- Q010: graph-walk retrieval outperforms vector-only by 20% on multi-hop QA (HippoRAG NeurIPS 2024)

**Core claim**: Cosine collapses temporal, contextual, causal, and novelty signals into a single semantic proximity scalar. Memory retrieval requires all four; search retrieval requires primarily semantic proximity. Cosine is correct for search and wrong in degree for memory — it captures one of four necessary signals and zero of three.

**Failure mode**: A mature Recall store returns the same 5 infrastructure memories every session (the highest-importance, most-cosine-similar memories to any session-start query), regardless of which task is active, because cosine has no temporal, causal, or novelty signal to differentiate retrieval by context.

**Falsifying condition for the attacker**: The attacker's case collapses when (a) the store is small and well-maintained (low redundancy, R < 0.10), so cosine's redundancy amplification is negligible, (b) queries are drawn from the same semantic domain as stored content (symmetric speech acts), (c) temporal and causal signals are low-variance (all memories are equally recent and causally unrelated), and (d) the store is cleaned of referential near-duplicates. Under these conditions, cosine with an importance multiplier is approximately the correct retrieval signal.

## Falsifying Conditions

### When the defender collapses:

1. **Redundancy accumulates** above R ≈ 0.20: cosine's top-K becomes dominated by paraphrase clusters. Q013 proves this occurs at R=0.40 in 92% of queries.

2. **Store scale exceeds ~5,000 memories**: JL lemma (Q013) shows 1024-dim embeddings only guarantee pairwise distance preservation within 26.8% distortion at N=10,000. Cosine scores become unreliable at scale.

3. **Queries are speech-act-asymmetric**: When queries are questions and stored content is statements, the geometric regions diverge. Cosine treats both as points in the same space.

4. **Referentially distinct but structurally similar facts coexist**: Any multi-host, multi-project, or multi-time-slice memory store will have "redis_host on host-A" and "redis_host on host-B" — cosine cannot distinguish them.

5. **Temporal recency matters**: Any task requiring knowledge of the current state of a changing entity (infrastructure, project status, debugging outcome) requires temporal ordering that cosine cannot provide.

### When the attacker collapses:

1. **The store is small and curated** (N < 500, R < 0.10): cosine's weaknesses are negligible at this scale.

2. **Alternative signals are unavailable**: If temporal metadata (`created_at`) is not stored, causal graph is not populated, and no feedback signal exists, cosine is the only tractable signal. A single imperfect signal beats zero.

3. **Queries are lookup-style, not causal**: For single-hop factual queries ("what is the IP address of the CasaOS box?"), cosine is near-optimal. The graph-walk advantage only materializes at multi-hop depth.

4. **The embedding model is domain-specialized**: A model fine-tuned on Recall's exact memory format and query patterns would organize the embedding space so that cosine more closely approximates the full utility signal. The attacker's case assumes a general-purpose embedding model.

## Synthesis

### What Thread A assumes that Thread B exposes

Thread A assumes the memory store is a static, curated, semantically diverse corpus — the conditions under which cosine was validated in production search. Thread B exposes that a personal AI memory store violates all three assumptions: it is dynamic (continuously updated), uncurated (all observations stored regardless of quality), and redundancy-accumulating (the same facts are stored many times in paraphrase form).

Thread A also assumes the retrieval task is "find text semantically related to the query" — the search definition. Thread B exposes that memory retrieval is "find the currently most useful observation given temporal, causal, and semantic context." These are genuinely different optimization targets.

**The hidden assumption cosine makes**: That the utility of a memory is a monotonic function of its semantic similarity to the query alone. This fails when utility also depends on recency (temporal decay), causal position in a chain (graph proximity), and marginal information gain over already-retrieved memories (novelty).

**The hidden assumption the attacker makes**: That cosine provides no useful signal for memory. This is wrong — semantic similarity is necessary, just not sufficient. Cosine is wrong in degree, not in kind.

### When Thread A is right

- Store size below ~2,000 memories with active deduplication (R < 0.15)
- Queries are primarily factual/lookup (single-hop)
- All memories are roughly equally recent (no temporal differentiation needed)
- The agent operates in a single context (no multi-machine, multi-project referential ambiguity)

### When Thread B is right

- Mature store (N > 5,000, R > 0.30)
- Causal or multi-hop queries dominate ("why did X break?", "what changed before Y?")
- Store contains infrastructure facts for multiple machines/projects (referential near-duplicates)
- The agent's most important task is tracking the *current state* of changing entities

### Wrong in kind or in degree?

**Wrong in degree, not in kind.** Cosine captures one of four necessary signals (semantic proximity) and zero of three (temporal, causal, novelty). It is not that cosine provides a wrong signal — it provides an incomplete signal. The correct retrieval function is cosine plus three additional signals, each of which addresses a distinct dimension of memory utility that cosine is structurally incapable of representing.

The analogy: a compass gives you bearing. That is correct and necessary for navigation. But bearing alone, without speed, altitude, and fuel level, is insufficient for flight navigation. Cosine is the bearing signal of memory retrieval — necessary, correct within its domain, but insufficient for the full problem.

### What does the correct signal look like?

A four-factor multiplicative scoring function applied to cosine-retrieved candidates:

```
final_score(m, q, t, K) = cosine_sim(m, q)^β           # Semantic — cosine as-is
                         × temporal_decay(age(m), t)^γ  # Temporal — power-law (Q006)
                         × ppr_score(m, q_entities)^δ   # Causal — PPR from Q010
                         × novelty(m, K)^ε              # Novelty — vs. already-retrieved set K
```

Where:
- `temporal_decay(age, t) = (1 + age_hours / τ)^(-α)` with τ=168h (1 week), α=0.5 (Ebbinghaus curve, Q014)
- `ppr_score` is the Personalized PageRank score from query-extracted entities through Neo4j (Q010)
- `novelty(m, K) = 1 - max_{m' ∈ K} cosine_sim(m, m')` — information gain relative to already-retrieved set
- β, γ, δ, ε are learned from feedback (which retrieved memories led to useful completions)

The practical implementation path: add signals incrementally. Cosine is the baseline. Temporal decay (γ > 0) is a 3-day implementation (Q006). Novelty re-ranking (ε > 0) is a 1-week implementation (re-rank the cosine candidate set by marginal diversity). PPR (δ > 0) is a 3-4 week implementation (Q010). Each signal adds independently without breaking the others.

## Ideas Extracted

### Idea: four-factor-memory-score
- **Novelty**: 0.90 — No production AI memory system combines all four signals (semantic, temporal, causal, novelty) in a single multiplicative scoring function. Each signal exists in isolation in various systems; the principled combination with learned exponents does not exist anywhere.
- **Evidence**: 0.75 — Each component has individual empirical support: cosine (production-validated), temporal decay (Ebbinghaus 1885 + LangChain production implementation), PPR (HippoRAG NeurIPS 2024, 20% improvement), novelty/MMR (Carbonell & Goldstein 1998, Maximal Marginal Relevance — validated in summarization and retrieval). The combination is architecturally motivated but not directly validated as a unit.
- **Feasibility**: 0.45 — Implementing all four components requires: (1) cosine — already done; (2) temporal decay — 3 days; (3) novelty re-ranking — 1 week; (4) PPR on Neo4j — 3-4 weeks; (5) exponent learning from feedback — 2-3 weeks. Total: 8-12 weeks for the full system. Can be staged: temporal + novelty in 2 weeks, PPR in month 2, learned exponents in month 3.
- **Description**: Replace Recall's current `final_score = cosine × importance` with a four-factor score applied to the top-50 cosine candidates returned from Qdrant: `score = cosine^β × temporal_decay^γ × ppr^δ × novelty^ε`. Start with fixed exponents (β=1.0, γ=0.3, δ=0.2, ε=0.2) tuned by hand. Log the final scores and which memories were used in completions. After accumulating 200+ scored retrievals, fit a logistic regression to learn the optimal exponents. The novelty factor `1 - max_{m' ∈ already_retrieved} cosine(m, m')` is computed greedily as memories are added to the returned set (MMR algorithm). This converts Recall's retrieval from a single-signal scalar to a multi-signal utility function.

### Idea: mmr-diversity-reranker
- **Novelty**: 0.60 — Maximal Marginal Relevance (MMR) is a well-known algorithm (Carbonell & Goldstein 1998); applying it to AI memory retrieval as a post-cosine diversity pass is known in some RAG systems but not in production AI memory systems (not in mem0, Zep, Letta, or Recall currently).
- **Evidence**: 0.85 — MMR is peer-reviewed and validated across summarization and retrieval tasks. The specific problem it solves (Q013: 92% of top-5 retrievals contain paraphrase duplicates) is directly measured for Recall's use case. MMR is the exact algorithm designed for this failure mode.
- **Feasibility**: 0.90 — MMR is a trivial O(K²) post-processing step applied to the top-50 cosine candidates. Implementation: retrieve top-50 from Qdrant by cosine similarity; run MMR with λ=0.7 (70% relevance, 30% diversity) to select top-5 from those 50. MMR picks the next memory as `argmax_m [λ × cosine(m, q) - (1-λ) × max_{m' ∈ selected} cosine(m, m')]`. Total implementation: 1-2 days. No infrastructure changes required — pure Python in the Recall FastAPI retrieval endpoint.
- **Description**: After Qdrant returns the top-50 cosine candidates, apply MMR selection to pick the final K=5 memories returned to the Claude context. MMR greedily selects memories that are simultaneously relevant to the query AND maximally different from already-selected memories. For Recall with R=0.40 redundancy: MMR will select 5 diverse facts rather than 5 paraphrases of the same fact. The λ parameter controls the relevance-diversity tradeoff (λ=1.0 is pure cosine; λ=0.0 is pure diversity). Start at λ=0.7. This is the single highest-ROI improvement available: 2 days of implementation, directly addresses the 92% paraphrase contamination finding from Q013.

### Idea: query-asymmetric-projection
- **Novelty**: 0.75 — Bi-encoder asymmetric similarity (DPR-style) exists in production search (Facebook DPR, Haystack); applying it to the specific case of AI memory where queries are natural language questions and stored items are hook-observed facts/events is novel at the production memory system level.
- **Evidence**: 0.70 — Karpukhin et al. 2020 (DPR) demonstrates measurable improvement in question-answer retrieval over symmetric similarity. The asymmetric speech act problem (Q014) is formally characterized: questions and statements occupy different geometric regions, and cosine treats them symmetrically when they should not be.
- **Feasibility**: 0.40 — Requires training two lightweight projection matrices: Q_proj (1024→512) applied to query embeddings, C_proj (1024→512) applied to stored memory embeddings. Training data: (query, useful_memory, not-useful_memory) triples extracted from Recall session logs where Claude's response referenced specific memories. Bootstrapping: use Claude to generate 200+ synthetic triples. Training: logistic regression or small MLP over 200 triples. Integration: store projected vectors alongside raw embeddings in Qdrant (or replace raw embeddings once validated). Implementation: 3-5 weeks including data collection and training.
- **Description**: Store two representations for each Recall memory: (1) the raw qwen3 embedding (for backward compatibility and raw cosine fallback), and (2) a projected 512-dim chunk embedding `C_proj @ raw_embedding`. At query time, embed the query as usual with qwen3, then apply the query projection: `q_proj = Q_proj @ raw_query_embedding`. Compute similarity as the inner product `q_proj · c_proj_memory`. The projection matrices are trained to push (query, correct_memory) pairs close together and (query, irrelevant_memory) pairs far apart in the projected space. This directly corrects the speech act asymmetry identified in Q014, where pure cosine undershoots similarity for (question, statement) pairs compared to (statement, statement) pairs.

### Idea: temporal-dimension-augmentation
- **Novelty**: 0.80 — Encoding temporal recency as an additional vector dimension appended to the embedding (making time a first-class ANN factor rather than a post-retrieval re-rank) is not implemented in any production system found in Q006 research. All systems apply time post-retrieval.
- **Evidence**: 0.65 — Q006 established that ANN algorithms (HNSW) cannot incorporate temporal decay directly because they use precomputed distances. The vector augmentation approach bypasses this limitation: by appending a time-encoding dimension to the vector, the L2/cosine metric naturally incorporates temporal decay without any algorithmic change. The sinusoidal time encoding approach is validated in transformer positional encoding literature; its application to retrieval vectors for temporal weighting is novel but mechanically sound.
- **Feasibility**: 0.60 — Implementation: at write time, append k=16 sinusoidal time-encoding dimensions to the 1024-dim embedding: `v_stored = concat(raw_embedding, sin(ω_i × age_at_write))` where age_at_write=0, then periodically re-embed memories with updated age (or use a reference time at query time). At query time, append k=16 zero-valued time dimensions (query has no age). The 16 appended dimensions pull the similarity score down for older memories proportionally to their age. Re-indexing Qdrant with 1040-dim vectors requires a collection migration. Implementation: 2-3 weeks. Risk: the temporal signal scale relative to the semantic signal must be calibrated — if the 16 temporal dimensions dominate, all old memories become unreachable regardless of semantic relevance.
- **Description**: Augment each stored Recall embedding from 1024 to 1040 dimensions by appending 16 sinusoidally-encoded temporal dimensions. The temporal encoding at write time is `[sin(2πf_i × 0), cos(2πf_i × 0)]` for 8 frequency pairs (f_i spanning days to years). As time passes, the encoding for a memory created at time t_0 becomes `[sin(2πf_i × (t_now - t_0)), cos(2πf_i × (t_now - t_0))]` — which would drift away from the query's zero-encoding, reducing cosine similarity with age. This makes temporal recency a first-class geometric property of the embedding space rather than an afterthought applied post-retrieval. The advantage over post-ANN re-ranking: the ANN traversal itself (HNSW graph walk) naturally deprioritizes older memories during candidate selection, not just during final ranking.

### Idea: importance-calibrated-cosine-threshold
- **Novelty**: 0.55 — Dynamic retrieval thresholds are known (Q002's ACh-model dynamic threshold); the specific calibration of the cosine floor threshold to the importance score distribution of the store (so that high-importance memories always clear the threshold and low-importance memories face a higher bar) is not found in any reviewed system.
- **Evidence**: 0.65 — The principle that high-importance memories should have higher recall (lower effective threshold) than low-importance memories is analogous to precision-recall tradeoffs in classification — lowering the threshold increases recall but hurts precision. For memory, the asymmetry is that missing a high-importance memory is a worse error than retrieving an irrelevant low-importance memory. This motivates importance-dependent thresholds. Q002 establishes the general principle; Q013 provides the specific failure mode (cosine retrieves paraphrases because no threshold exists to reject near-identical memories already retrieved).
- **Feasibility**: 0.80 — Pure scoring change in the Recall FastAPI retrieval endpoint. No infrastructure changes. After Qdrant returns top-50 candidates, compute `effective_threshold(m) = base_threshold × (1 + novelty_penalty) / (1 + importance_bonus)` where `novelty_penalty = max_{already_selected} cosine(m, m')` and `importance_bonus = importance_score(m)`. This is a lightweight algebraic modification to the current `final_score = cosine × importance` formula, adding the novelty penalty as a denominator adjustment. Implementation: 1-2 days.
- **Description**: Modify Recall's retrieval re-ranking to use an importance-calibrated effective threshold that raises the bar for redundant memories and lowers it for high-importance memories. Formula: retain a memory from the candidate set if `cosine_sim(m, q) × importance(m) / max_{m' ∈ selected} cosine(m, m') > base_threshold`. For a memory with no near-duplicates already selected, `max cosine = 0` → standard threshold. For a memory that is 0.95 cosine-similar to an already-selected memory, the denominator pushes its effective score down by 1/0.95 ≈ 1.05x — it must be that much better to clear the threshold. High-importance memories get a bonus in the numerator. This is a lightweight version of MMR that uses the existing importance score rather than adding a new parameter.

## Suggested Follow-ups

- **[PHYSICS] Measure MMR improvement empirically**: Export 100 recent Recall session queries and their top-5 cosine retrievals. For each, compute the pairwise cosine similarity among the returned set. Average pairwise cosine > 0.85 confirms Q013's paraphrase contamination. Then apply MMR with λ=0.7 and measure the average pairwise similarity of the new top-5. This is a one-afternoon measurement that directly validates whether MMR is needed.

- **[ABSENCE] Q028-followup — Does any AI memory system use MMR?**: The Q013 finding showed 92% paraphrase contamination. MMR is a 1978 algorithm. The absence of MMR in mem0, Zep, Letta, and Recall is striking. Is there a documented reason it hasn't been adopted, or is this genuinely a blind spot?

- **[ADVERSARIAL] Q031 — Is importance scoring the correct signal, or does it amplify the wrong memories?**: Recall's current formula is `cosine × importance`. If importance is correlated with frequency-of-storage (memories that appear often get high importance), then high-importance memories are precisely the high-redundancy memories that MMR would down-weight. The importance signal and the novelty signal may be anti-correlated — which should win?

- **[TABOO] Q032 — What if cosine were removed entirely from the retrieval path for a 30-day trial?**: Use only temporal decay (retrieve the 10 most recent memories filtered by keyword match) and measure whether Claude's session quality degrades. This falsifies or confirms whether cosine is load-bearing at current Recall store sizes (likely < 5,000 memories).

- **[ADJACENT] Q033 — Maximal Marginal Relevance was developed for multi-document summarization. Does its tradeoff parameter λ need to be tuned differently for AI memory vs. document summarization?**: In summarization, λ=0.5 is standard. For memory, where the goal is maximum coverage of distinct facts rather than maximum summarization quality, λ might optimally be lower (more diversity, less relevance). What does the optimal λ look like for Recall's retrieval use case?
