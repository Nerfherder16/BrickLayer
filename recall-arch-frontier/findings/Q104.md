# Finding: Q104 — Reclassification Job Failure: Classification Drift Rate, Impact Timeline, and Detection

**Question**: What happens to retrieval quality when the CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH reclassification background job stops running — specifically, does the graph silently accumulate stale-classified FRESH edges (reinforced edges that have aged past tau but remain labeled FRESH), what is the rate of classification drift at Tim's usage density, and what health signal or alarm would detect this failure mode before it causes material retrieval degradation?
**Question Type**: ADVERSARIAL
**Verdict**: BREAKTHROUGH
**Severity**: High
**Source field**: temporal graph partitioning, database background job failure modes, precision degradation in approximate retrieval systems, monitoring theory (Type I/II error tradeoffs in health signals)

---

## Adversarial Analysis: CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH job failure consequences

---

### Thread A: Defense — The job failing is catastrophic

**Core claim**: After tau_edge sessions (30 epochs) of job silence, every CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH edge in the graph is stale — BFS returns the entire historical graph as "fresh," precision collapses to near-zero, and the system becomes epistemically unsafe (injecting years-old associations into current context).

**Evidence and mechanism**:

The reclassification job's purpose is to enforce the invariant: every CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH edge satisfies `last_reinforced_epoch >= current_epoch - tau_edge`. When the job stops, that invariant is violated for every edge that ages past the threshold. The key graph dynamic:

- Tim produces ~45 new CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH edges per session.
- Each of those edges will age past tau_edge=30 after 30 sessions.
- Simultaneously, no FRESH edges are being reclassified to STALE.
- The FRESH chain therefore grows by 45 edges/session and shrinks by 0.
- After N sessions of silence, the FRESH chain contains edges from the current session back through the (N + tau_edge)th session — a window that expands indefinitely rather than being bounded at tau_edge.

**Accumulation table (job stopped at session 0)**:

| Sessions elapsed | Stale edges in FRESH chain | Total FRESH chain size | Fresh-window edges | Precision (correct/total) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0 | F_fresh_steady_state | F_fresh | 1.00 |
| 1 | ~45 | F_fresh + 45 | F_fresh | ~0.95 |
| 5 | ~225 | F_fresh + 225 | F_fresh | ~0.77 |
| 10 | ~450 | F_fresh + 450 | F_fresh | ~0.62 |
| 20 | ~900 | F_fresh + 900 | F_fresh | ~0.40 |
| 30 | ~1,350 | F_fresh + 1350 | F_fresh | ~0.27 |
| 60 | ~2,700 | F_fresh + 2700 | F_fresh | ~0.16 |

Where F_fresh_steady_state = tau_edge × 45 = 30 × 45 = 1,350 edges (the expected FRESH chain at steady state, before the job failure). Precision = F_fresh / (F_fresh + stale_accumulated).

After 30 sessions (= 30 days at one session/day), the stale accumulation matches the entire steady-state FRESH chain size. Precision has halved. The BFS traversal now explores twice as many nodes as intended — every stale edge drags in a neighbor node that was relevant 30–60 sessions ago, not the current context.

**Best case scenario**: The job resumes after exactly one missed session. The single-epoch drift (45 stale edges in FRESH chain) is minor — precision drops from 1.00 to ~0.97 for that session. Q096's "bounded to one epoch" guarantee holds in the optimistic case. But this optimism requires the job to miss at most one session, which cannot be assumed when the job has stopped.

**Latency amplification compound effect**: The type-partitioning architecture's core value (15× speedup from reading F_fresh instead of F_total) erodes as the FRESH chain grows. If the FRESH chain is 3× larger than intended (from 30 missed sessions), the BFS no longer achieves O(F_fresh) — it achieves O(F_fresh + stale_accumulated) ≈ O(F_fresh × 3). The 15× speedup degrades toward 5×, then toward 1×, as the accumulated stale edges grow without bound.

At 180 sessions of silence (6 months of daily usage), the FRESH chain contains every CO_RETRIEVED edge ever written. The graph partition has completely collapsed. The BFS is now equivalent to the pre-migration full-chain traversal — same cost, same retrieval window — except the system believes it is running the optimized partitioned architecture.

**The silence is the danger**: No error is thrown. The BFS query executes and returns results. The count of retrieved neighbors increases over time, but this looks like normal organic graph growth. There is no crash, no exception, no obvious signal that the reclassification job has stopped.

### Thread B: Optimist — The job failing is a manageable precision erosion, not a corruption event

**Core claim**: Stale FRESH edges expand the working set, but they represent real memories that were genuinely fresh tau_edge sessions ago — they are not corrupted data, not injected falsehoods, and not semantically invalid. The S5 signal that monitors retrieval compliance will not detect this failure mode specifically, but the impact is bounded, gradual, and qualitatively different from the orphan injection problem.

**Evidence and mechanism**:

The fundamental distinction between this failure mode and the orphan injection failure (Q100):

- **Orphan injection**: Qdrant returns a UUID for a deleted memory. The Python layer joins to PostgreSQL and gets no row (or a logically-deleted row). The memory returned to Claude is either fabricated (wrong UUID) or empty (deleted content). This is **content corruption**: Claude receives content that does not exist.

- **FRESH chain drift**: The BFS returns additional neighbors via stale FRESH edges. Those neighbors are real Memory nodes with real content in Qdrant and PostgreSQL. The content is correct. The issue is **temporal relevance**: the associations being traversed were reinforced more than tau_edge sessions ago, meaning they represent co-retrieval patterns from old contexts, not the current working context.

The question is: how harmful is it to inject an older association rather than no association?

**The forgetting curve argument**: tau_edge=30 was set to mean "associations reinforced within the last 30 sessions are relevant." An edge reinforced 35 sessions ago would not be traversed in the correct system. In the failed system, it is traversed. That 35-session-old edge connects two memories that were co-retrieved in a context from 5+ weeks ago. In many cases — specifically when those memories remain semantically related to the current query — this is not a retrieval error at all. The association is old but not wrong.

The retrieval degradation is probabilistic: only the fraction of stale edges that connect to nodes not already in the FRESH-neighborhood does damage. If the same memory pairs that were co-retrieved 35 sessions ago are still semantically relevant to Tim's current queries, the stale FRESH edge is delivering correct results (memories that would have been retrieved by the vector similarity path anyway). The edge is stale in the epoch-window sense but correct in the semantic sense.

**Quantifying the overlap**: At steady state, the FRESH chain (tau_edge=30 sessions) contains the most recently reinforced pairs. The stale drift adds pairs from session 31, 32, etc. ago. If Tim's query patterns are consistent (he tends to work on the same projects and topics over months), the pairs from session 31–35 ago are probably the same memories he would co-retrieve today. The "precision loss" is overestimated by the naive precision calculation because it assumes stale pairs are wrong, when many of them are still correct given semantic overlap.

**Practical impact bound**: At Tim's density (1-3 LLPC calls per session, top-10 retrieval), the BFS from each seed expands to neighbors. With 45 extra stale edges per missed session and ~50-150 fresh neighbors at steady state, the first few missed sessions add neighbors proportionally. The LLM context window injected by Recall is bounded by design (top-K results, typically K=10-20). Even with a 50% inflated FRESH chain, if the injected results are still sorted by co_retrieval_count DESC, the most strongly reinforced pairs rise to the top regardless of whether they are "truly fresh" — a pair co-retrieved 40 times in the last year outranks a pair co-retrieved once 28 sessions ago, even though the once-recent pair is technically FRESH and the other technically STALE.

**S5 signal behavior**: The S5 health probe (from Q092) monitors whether orphaned memory UUIDs are being returned in BFS results — specifically, whether BFS traversal yields nodes not present in Qdrant. Stale FRESH edges do not cause this: their endpoint nodes are real Memory nodes with valid Qdrant vectors. S5 remains green throughout FRESH chain drift. The existing monitoring infrastructure produces no alarm for this failure mode. This is concerning from a detection standpoint but it means the system is not exhibiting the dangerous pattern S5 was designed to catch.

**Failure mode**: The slow precision erosion becomes user-perceptible only after many sessions of drift — and even then, Tim's experience would be "Recall surfaces older associations more than I'd like," not "Recall is giving me wrong information." This is qualitatively less severe than content corruption.

### Synthesis

**What Thread A assumes that Thread B exposes**:

Thread A implicitly assumes that all stale-epoch edges are semantically irrelevant. This assumption is false: an edge reinforced 35 sessions ago between memory nodes A and B does not mean the A-B relationship has expired — it means the specific working context in which A and B co-occurred is no longer the current context. If Tim returns to a similar working context (same project, same technology stack, same problem domain), the A-B association is semantically fresh even if epoch-stale. Thread A's precision calculation (stale edges / total edges) is a worst-case upper bound on actual retrieval degradation, not a realized degradation rate.

Thread A also assumes the FRESH chain grows without bound as the job stops. This is correct for new edges, but overlooks the natural bound from the memory expiry process: as Tim's Memory nodes age past tau (the node-level epoch filter in the BFS WHERE clause), they become invisible to retrieval anyway. Stale FRESH edges connecting to aged-out nodes contribute zero retrieval pollution — the node predicate `WHERE neighbor.last_access_epoch >= ($current_epoch - $tau)` filters them out at query time even if the edge type says FRESH.

Thread B assumes strong semantic overlap between current and past queries. This assumption is context-dependent: if Tim changes projects or domains, old associations from the previous domain become genuine false retrievals. Thread B's argument holds when Tim's work is continuous; it breaks when Tim pivots to a new project.

**When Thread A is right**:
- When Tim pivots to a distinctly new domain or project after an extended run in a different domain
- When the reclassification job has been silent for >15 sessions (precision < 0.75 even under generous overlap assumptions)
- When the graph is in a phase of rapid growth (many new memory nodes, new associations dominating the working set) — old stale edges become proportionally more polluting
- When the node-level epoch filter (tau) is relaxed upward, removing the natural backstop that filters aged-out neighbors

**When Thread B is right**:
- When Tim's work patterns are consistent across sessions (same stack, same projects)
- When the reclassification job has been silent for fewer than 10 sessions (precision > 0.75)
- When co_retrieval_count ordering keeps the most-reinforced pairs at the top of BFS results regardless of edge staleness
- When tau (node staleness) is tight enough to filter aged-out nodes, limiting how far stale FRESH edges can propagate in the graph

**The architecture Thread B would accept**:

The core finding synthesized from both threads: the reclassification job failure has **two distinct degradation phases** with a sharp boundary at session N=tau_edge (30 sessions):

**Phase 1 (0–tau_edge sessions of silence)**: The stale FRESH edges connect to Memory nodes that are still within the node epoch window (they were reinforced less than tau_edge epochs ago, so their last_access_epoch is also recent). The node-level predicate `WHERE neighbor.last_access_epoch >= ($current_epoch - $tau)` does NOT filter them — they pass both the edge-type check (FRESH) and the node freshness check. This is the window of genuine retrieval degradation. Precision falls from 1.00 toward ~0.50 over this window.

**Phase 2 (>tau_edge sessions of silence)**: Stale FRESH edges are now connected to Memory nodes that are themselves aging past the tau node window. As these nodes age, the node-level predicate begins filtering them out — the stale FRESH edges lead to nodes that the BFS rejects anyway. The effective retrieval pollution stabilizes and then decreases as the node-level filter catches what the job failure missed at the edge level. The FRESH chain is still inflated, but many of those extra traversals are wasted (read the edge, follow to node, evaluate node predicate, reject) rather than injecting a wrong neighbor.

This creates a paradox: **Phase 1 is worse for retrieval precision but better for detection** (the stale edges deliver results, and a precision-aware health metric could catch the growing result set). **Phase 2 is better for retrieval precision** (node filter backstops the edge drift) **but the FRESH chain is still inflated** (BFS traversal cost has grown without the retrieval benefit, eroding the 15× speedup but not injecting wrong results).

**The minimal fix (Thread B would accept, Thread A demands)**:

The detection mechanism that neither thread independently proposed: a **FRESH-chain size monitor** that checks the ratio of total FRESH edges to the expected steady-state count.

Expected FRESH chain size at steady state: `tau_edge × sessions_per_epoch × edges_per_session ≈ 30 × 1 × 45 = 1,350 edges`. If the job is running correctly, the FRESH chain oscillates around this value (±10% due to variance in co-retrieval rates).

If the job stops, the FRESH chain grows by ~45 edges/session. After 10 sessions, FRESH count = ~1,800 (33% above expected). After 20 sessions, FRESH count = ~2,250 (67% above expected).

Alert threshold: FRESH count > 1.5 × expected_steady_state (i.e., > 2,025 edges at Tim's rate). At this threshold, the job has been silent for approximately 15 sessions — Phase 1 degradation is active, but Phase 2 backstop has not yet engaged.

Detection Cypher (runs as part of session-end health check):

```cypher
MATCH ()-[r:CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH]->()
RETURN count(r) AS fresh_total,
       count(CASE WHEN r.last_reinforced_epoch < ($current_epoch - $tau_edge) THEN 1 END) AS stale_classified_as_fresh,
       toFloat(count(CASE WHEN r.last_reinforced_epoch < ($current_epoch - $tau_edge) THEN 1 END)) /
       toFloat(count(r)) AS drift_fraction
```

This single query surfaces three actionable signals:
1. `fresh_total` — absolute count of FRESH edges (growing trend signals job failure)
2. `stale_classified_as_fresh` — direct count of the classification error (the S5-adjacent metric Q096 named "fresh_leakage_count")
3. `drift_fraction` — the fraction of FRESH chain that is misclassified (alarm if > 0.25)

The `stale_classified_as_fresh` count is the authoritative signal: it is exactly zero when the job runs correctly, and grows monotonically at ~45/session when the job fails. At Tim's scale (1,350 FRESH edges at steady state), a threshold of 200 stale-classified-as-fresh edges corresponds to approximately 4.4 missed sessions — early enough to catch the failure before Phase 1 degradation becomes severe (precision ~0.87 at 5 missed sessions).

**Q096 already specified this threshold** (alert at >200), but framed it as the "leakage detector" rather than as the primary job health signal. The synthesis elevates it: `stale_classified_as_fresh > 200` is the **primary health alarm** for this failure mode, replacing the absent S5 detection, and it must be surfaced in the dashboard alongside the S5 metric.

## Ideas Extracted

### Idea: fresh-chain-size-trend-monitor
- **Novelty**: 0.74 — Monitoring the total count of CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH edges as a time-series health signal (trend detection, not just threshold) is not implemented in any reviewed AI memory system. The insight that FRESH chain growth rate encodes reclassification job health — without needing to inspect the job process directly — is a structural consequence of the type-partitioning architecture. This monitoring approach would not exist without the FRESH/STALE partition; it is a new observability primitive that the architecture enables.
- **Evidence**: 0.72 — Follows directly from the accumulation model: 45 edges/session × missed sessions = FRESH chain growth. The math is straightforward and derivable from Q096 parameters. Analogous to LSM-tree tombstone accumulation monitoring in RocksDB (the "space amplification" metric that signals compaction debt). In both cases, a structure that should be bounded grows unboundedly when the cleanup process fails, and the growth rate is a direct signal of the failure.
- **Feasibility**: 0.92 — Single Cypher count query at session end. Can be added to the existing S5 health probe execution path (runs alongside the S5 BFS sampling query). Returns fresh_total in <10ms at Tim's scale. Add to the dashboard as a trend sparkline next to the existing S5 signal. Alert threshold: fresh_total > 1.5 × (tau_edge × avg_edges_per_session). Requires storing a running average of edges_per_session (computed from results.tsv or Redis counter). Total implementation: ~20 lines of code in the session-end hook.
- **Description**: A time-series monitor for the total CO_RETRIEVED_FRESH edge count, collected at each session end. Expected value: tau_edge × avg_edges_per_session ≈ 1,350 edges at Tim's rate. Alert: absolute count > 1.5× expected steady-state OR growth rate > 2× sessions_per_week for 3 consecutive sessions. This detects reclassification job failure before Phase 1 precision degradation reaches material levels (~5-10 missed sessions), without requiring any process monitoring of the session-end hook itself. The signal is derived purely from graph state.

### Idea: drift-fraction-as-primary-health-metric
- **Novelty**: 0.79 — Using `stale_classified_as_fresh / total_fresh` as the canonical health metric for the type-partitioning architecture — rather than a job process monitor or a count threshold — is a precision-proxy signal derived from graph state. It is not found in reviewed graph database operational literature. The key novelty: this metric is a direct measure of the invariant violation rate. In a correctly-running system, it should be 0 immediately after reclassification runs. A threshold on drift_fraction converts an implementation correctness property (invariant holds) into an operational alarm (invariant is N% violated).
- **Evidence**: 0.70 — Q096's leakage detector query is the foundation; this idea extends it to a ratio metric. The drift fraction is a better alarm than the absolute count (stale_classified_as_fresh > 200) because it is invariant to graph scale — as the graph grows from 1,350 to 5,000 FRESH edges over months of usage, the absolute threshold of 200 becomes less meaningful (200/5000 = 4% drift is minor; 200/1350 = 15% drift is significant). A fraction-based threshold (e.g., drift_fraction > 0.15) scales correctly with graph growth.
- **Feasibility**: 0.92 — The Q096 leakage detector query already returns the count. Adding the fraction is a single Cypher expression change (`toFloat(stale_count) / toFloat(total_count)`). No new infrastructure. Alert threshold: drift_fraction > 0.15 (corresponding to ~3-4 missed sessions at Tim's rate). Can be surfaced in the dashboard as a gauge (0.00–0.20 healthy range, 0.20+ alert).
- **Description**: Extend Q096's leakage detector to compute drift_fraction = stale_classified_as_fresh / total_fresh at each session end. This is the canonical health signal for the reclassification job: 0.00 = perfect invariant, 0.15 = ~3 missed sessions (warn), 0.25 = ~5 missed sessions (alert, Phase 1 precision degradation active). Unlike absolute count thresholds, drift_fraction scales with graph growth and remains meaningful throughout the system lifetime. Surfaced in the Recall dashboard alongside the S5 orphan-miss rate.

### Idea: node-epoch-filter-as-natural-backstop
- **Novelty**: 0.68 — The observation that the BFS node-level epoch predicate (`WHERE neighbor.last_access_epoch >= ($current_epoch - $tau)`) acts as a natural backstop for FRESH chain drift — filtering out stale FRESH edges whose endpoints have themselves aged past the node epoch window — is not made explicit in Q091 or Q096. This is an emergent property of the dual-predicate architecture (edge-type freshness + node-epoch freshness) that limits the damage from reclassification job failure to a bounded window (Phase 1: 0 to tau sessions of silence). After tau sessions of silence, the node predicate begins reclaiming the degradation. This suggests tau and tau_edge should be co-designed with the reclassification job's failure tolerance in mind.
- **Evidence**: 0.74 — Derivable from the cost model: a stale FRESH edge connects memory A (seed) to memory B (neighbor). For B to appear in BFS results, it must pass `WHERE neighbor.last_access_epoch >= ($current_epoch - $tau)`. If tau=20 and the edge aged out of tau_edge=30, the edge became stale at epoch E_stale. Memory B must have a last_access_epoch >= (current_epoch - 20). If B was last accessed at the same epoch as the edge's last reinforcement (typical: the edge is reinforced when both A and B are retrieved together), then B's last_access_epoch ≈ E_stale as well. At epoch E_stale + 20, B ages past the node window. So the node predicate begins filtering the problematic neighbors approximately tau=20 sessions after each stale edge was reinforced.
- **Feasibility**: 0.95 — No code required. This is an architectural observation about the interaction between tau_edge (edge window) and tau (node window). It informs the co-design principle: tau_edge > tau guarantees that every stale FRESH edge whose endpoint has not been accessed since the reinforcement event will be filtered by the node predicate before Phase 2 ends. The recommendation: maintain tau_edge > tau + N_tolerated_missed_sessions to ensure the node backstop engages before unrecovered drift causes permanent precision loss.
- **Description**: The BFS node-level epoch predicate acts as a safety net for reclassification job failures. When a FRESH edge becomes stale (reinforced more than tau_edge sessions ago), its endpoint node typically also ages past the node window (tau sessions) within tau additional sessions. After tau sessions of silence, the node predicate begins filtering out the stale FRESH edge's endpoints, reducing effective retrieval pollution. This means Phase 1 degradation is bounded in duration (not magnitude) by tau: the system partially self-heals via the node filter, even without the reclassification job running. Design implication: tau_edge should exceed tau by at least the number of reclassification job misses that constitute an acceptable risk.

## Impact Timeline Summary

At Tim's density (45 edges/session, tau_edge=30, tau=20, assuming 1 session/day):

| Missed sessions | Stale-classified FRESH | drift_fraction | Phase | Retrieval impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 45 | 0.03 | Phase 1 | Negligible; ~45 extra neighbors in BFS, most legitimate |
| 4 | 180 | 0.12 | Phase 1 | Q096 alert threshold approached; precision ~0.89 |
| 5 | 225 | 0.14 | Phase 1 | drift_fraction alarm fires (>0.15); precision ~0.87 |
| 10 | 450 | 0.25 | Phase 1 | Serious; precision ~0.75; BFS traversal ~2× intended cost |
| 20 | 900 | 0.40 | Phase 1/2 boundary | Node filter begins engaging; effective pollution slows |
| 30 | 1,350 | 0.50 | Phase 2 | FRESH chain is 2× expected; but many stale endpoints filtered by node predicate |
| 60 | 2,700 | 0.67 | Phase 2 | Traversal cost degraded; retrieval precision partially restored by node filter |

**Detection fires at session 5 (drift_fraction > 0.15)**, before Phase 1 precision degradation reaches material levels (< 0.85). This is the recommended alarm threshold.

**The S5 signal remains green throughout**: stale FRESH edges connect to valid Memory nodes; S5 detects orphan misses (invalid UUIDs), not epoch-window violations.
