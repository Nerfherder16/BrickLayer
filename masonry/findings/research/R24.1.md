# Finding: R24.1 — Phase 17 Metric Changes Will Not Reach 75-80% Ceiling

**Question ID**: R24.1
**Status**: DONE
**Verdict**: WARNING
**Severity**: High
**Confidence**: 0.88
**Agent**: research-analyst
**Date**: 2026-03-22

## Summary

The four Phase 17 metric changes are directionally correct but not sufficient to reach 75-80%. The true ceiling constraint is verdict accuracy (~35-45%), not evidence scoring. Two changes are decorative noise, one actively penalizes valid findings, and the filter removes 16.9% of training data without proportional signal gain. Realistic target: 70-73%.

## Evidence

### Current Metric Decomposition

`build_metric()` in `optimizer.py` lines 23-68 scores three components:
- Verdict match: 0.4 weight, binary (0 or 0.4)
- Evidence quality: 0.4 weight — 1.0 if `len(evidence) > 100`, 0.5 otherwise (partial credit for short)
- Confidence calibration: 0.2 weight — `1 - |predicted_conf - 0.75|`

The reported 68.3% (best trial, Wave 23) vs 66.07% (full eval) is the **composite metric score**, not verdict accuracy. Decomposing at 66.07%:

```
0.4 * V_rate + 0.4 * ev_quality + 0.2 * calib = 0.6607

If ev_quality ≈ 0.90 (model generates >100 chars reliably):
  0.4 * V_rate = 0.6607 - 0.36 - 0.16 = 0.1407
  V_rate ≈ 35%  (true verdict accuracy at full eval)

If ev_quality ≈ 0.85:
  V_rate ≈ 40%
```

**The score of 66.07% reflects a model that gets verdicts right only ~35-40% of the time.** The evidence and calibration components provide a floor of ~0.52-0.56 even when the verdict is wrong (because evidence is almost always >100 chars and calibration at mean conf 0.947 gives `1 - |0.947 - 0.75| = 0.803`, contributing `0.2 × 0.803 = 0.16`). This floor compresses the metric range such that a +5-8 point gain requires fundamentally different optimization — not metric tweaks.

The 3 bootstrapped demos in `quantitative-analyst.json` all contain numbers, thresholds, and percentage values — they already pass the proposed content signal. This confirms the optimized prompt was shaped around substantive evidence, not that the proposed signal is novel.

### Analysis of Each Planned Improvement

#### 1. Replace length check with content signals (numbers + quantitative language + 300 chars min)

**Impact: Marginal positive signal, net metric reduction**

- Current: 83.5% of research findings get full evidence credit (>100 chars)
- Proposed: 71.4% get full credit (numbers AND >300 chars), 16.9% get zero credit
- Net change: 11.7% of findings lose full credit, 16.9% go from partial (0.5 → 0.0)

The new zero-credit findings are not garbage: they include D1.1 (2,362-char structural code analysis with no `$` or `%` patterns), D1.6 (3,673 chars), D10.1 (1,834 chars). These are legitimate FAILURE/HEALTHY findings with descriptive rather than numerical evidence. The new signal correctly rewards quantitative research findings but incorrectly penalizes qualitative structural findings that are equally valid outputs for diagnose-analyst and research-analyst.

**Gradient signal improvement**: Modest. The content signal discriminates better within the research-analyst corpus, but 28.6% of valid findings now get sub-full credit, lowering the metric ceiling.

**Threshold check**: The ROADMAP criterion was that this change affects ≥30% of training records with correct directional signal. Evidence: 28.6% of records are affected (16.9% zero credit + 11.7% partial shift). Marginally meets the 30% threshold but the directional signal is mixed — it penalizes qualitative findings alongside substantively weak ones.

#### 2. Add severity validation (0.15 weight)

**Impact: Noise addition, minimal discrimination**

- 91.1% of research findings have valid severity (Low/Medium/High/Info)
- 8.9% have MISSING (unfilled template fields, synthesis files)
- The model already generates severity reliably; this adds easy points

Severity validation adds an average of `0.15 × 0.911 = 0.137` per example to the metric. However, unless the weights are renormalized (total would become verdict(0.4) + evidence(0.4) + severity(0.15) + calib(0.2) = 1.15), the metric inflates without improving discrimination. The ROADMAP does not specify whether other weights are adjusted. If evidence weight drops to 0.25 (summing to 1.0), the mean metric score decreases because evidence discrimination is weakened more than severity adds.

Severity is not independent of verdict: all HEALTHY findings in the sample have Low/Info severity, all FAILURE findings have High/Medium. Severity is partly redundant with verdict — a severity validator that always returns 1.0 when the LM generates any valid label adds ~0.14 to every score uniformly, compressing discrimination rather than improving it.

**Threshold check**: Not a meaningful signal. Fails the independence test.

#### 3. Verdict-conditioned confidence calibration (HEALTHY→0.85, WARNING→0.75, FAILURE→0.70)

**Impact: Actively penalizes FAILURE findings, tiny net change**

Training data confidence by verdict:
- HEALTHY: n=20, mean confidence=0.958, current calib=0.792, proposed calib=0.892 (+0.10)
- WARNING: n=26, mean confidence=0.926, current calib=0.824, proposed calib=0.824 (neutral)
- FAILURE: n=31, mean confidence=0.958, current calib=0.792, proposed calib=0.742 (-0.05)

FAILURE findings constitute 39.2% of the research training set and have mean confidence 0.958. The proposed target for FAILURE is 0.70. This means the most common finding type in the training data gets a **lower calibration score** than today (`1 - |0.958 - 0.70| = 0.742` vs `1 - |0.958 - 0.75| = 0.792`). The optimizer will be pushed to generate lower-confidence FAILURE findings, which may or may not be appropriate for quantitative simulation questions.

Net mean calibration change: 0.8029 → 0.8087 (+0.006) — essentially zero at the macro level.

**Threshold check**: The criterion "does verdict-conditioned calibration produce different targets than fixed 0.75 for ≥20% of records?" — yes, FAILURE cases (39.2%) get a different target. But the direction is regressive for FAILURE findings.

#### 4. Filter training records with score < 0.4

**Impact: Cleans noise but over-reduces training set**

Under the current metric, a finding scores < 0.4 only if the verdict prediction is wrong AND evidence is short AND calibration is low. In practice, evidence is rarely short (83.5% pass >100 chars) so the threshold is rarely hit even on wrong-verdict predictions. With the proposed metric using stricter evidence signals, 13 findings (16.9%) would score near-zero purely because they have empty evidence fields.

The 13 empty-evidence research findings span verdicts: 7 HEALTHY, 4 WARNING, 2 FAILURE. Removing them reduces the training set from 77 to ~64 (-17%). At a starting corpus of 77 research-type findings, a 17% reduction may impair MIPROv2 bootstrapping: with `max_bootstrapped_demos=3`, the optimizer needs at least 3-5 usable examples per verdict class. After filtering, FAILURE n=29, WARNING n=22, HEALTHY n=13 — the HEALTHY class becomes thin.

**Threshold check**: The filter removes real noise (empty evidence is a training defect) but the dataset is too small to absorb a 17% cut gracefully. The simultaneous search space expansion (num_trials 10→20, max_bootstrapped_demos 2→5) requires more data, not less.

### Bootstrapped Demos — Do They Validate the Content Signal?

All 3 demos in `quantitative-analyst.json` contain numbers and threshold language. Demo 1 contains: `treasury_yr1_usd: $1,731,395`, `monthly_breakeven: 219 employees`, percentages. Demo 2 contains tables with dollar amounts, `$29,468` per employee calculations. Demo 3 contains `purchasing_power_ratio: 0.284x`, `flywheel_velocity: 4.8x`.

These demos already embody the content signal. The optimizer found them via the current (blunt) metric because they are long (1,000-3,000 chars) which passes the >100 char threshold. Switching to the proposed signal would still select these demos — but the proposed signal would not select them _more_ than the current one does. The demos are already the top-quality examples and would score identically at the top.

### Metric Ceiling Calculation

The theoretical ceiling for the current metric (perfect verdict prediction, evidence always >100 chars, mean calibration):
```
0.40 + 0.40 + 0.2 × (1 - |0.947 - 0.75|) = 0.40 + 0.40 + 0.161 = 0.961 (96.1%)
```

The gap between the theoretical ceiling (96.1%) and achieved score (66.07%) is not a metric calibration problem — it is a **verdict prediction accuracy problem**. The metric ceiling of 75-80% requires the model to reach ~65-75% verdict accuracy (assuming evidence generation stays high). The Phase 17 changes do not address verdict accuracy.

What would move verdict accuracy from ~35-40% to ~65-75%:
- Better question-text → context pairing in training data (most examples have `project_context` from project-brief.md, but many have empty `question_text` because the `_build_qid_to_agent_map` table-format gap drops attribution — see D24.1 finding)
- More training examples with diverse verdicts (currently FAILURE=39.2%, HEALTHY=25.3%, WARNING=32.9% — slight FAILURE skew)
- Clearer verdict definitions in the DSPy instruction prompt (current instruction in `quantitative-analyst.json` is generic and does not define when HEALTHY vs WARNING vs FAILURE applies)

## Threshold Analysis

- Relevant threshold: ROADMAP Phase 17 target of 75-80% metric score
- Current ceiling: 68.3% (best trial) / 66.07% (full eval)
- Evidence suggests achievable with Phase 17 changes: 69-73% (+1 to +5 points)
- Gap to 75% lower bound: -2 to -6 points below target
- Gap to 80% upper bound: -7 to -11 points below target

The +5-8 point ROADMAP projection is based on the assumption that metric improvements directly translate to optimization gains. The evidence shows the verdict accuracy floor (~35-40%) is the binding constraint — metric tweaks that redistribute evidence/severity/calibration weights without improving the verdict prediction gradient will yield +1-3 points of compression effect, not the +5-8 points projected.

## Confidence

Evidence quality: HIGH
Reasoning: All findings are drawn from direct code reads (`optimizer.py`, `training_extractor.py`, `quantitative-analyst.json`), quantitative analysis of the full 164-finding corpus (79 research-type), and mathematical decomposition of the 66.07% metric score. The verdict accuracy derivation is a constraint inference (not a direct measurement) but is mathematically bounded by the known score components.

## What Would Change This Verdict

- HEALTHY: If a re-run with the Phase 17 metric achieves ≥75% in at least one trial, the projection is validated regardless of the component analysis here
- FAILURE (regression): If the severity validation check is implemented with case-sensitive matching and the training data uses mixed-case severity values (e.g. "medium" vs "Medium"), the 91.1% pass rate drops to 0% — a silent regression to a lower metric ceiling than today
- HEALTHY (revised mechanism): If the `_build_qid_to_agent_map` table-format gap (D24.1) is fixed simultaneously, restoring proper question_text attribution to training records, verdict accuracy could jump to 60%+ independently of metric changes — and then Phase 17 changes would compound to reach 75-80%

## Verdict Rationale

The Phase 17 metric improvements are a WARNING because they address a real but secondary problem (evidence quality discrimination) while leaving the primary bottleneck untouched (verdict prediction accuracy of ~35-40%). Two of the four changes (severity validation, verdict-conditioned confidence) either add redundant signal or actively penalize valid findings. The content signal replacement is the only change with meaningful discriminative value, but it also introduces a new zero-credit category that will penalize 16.9% of training examples. The combined effect is a realistic +1-4 point gain, falling short of the 75-80% target by at least 2-7 points.

The 75-80% target is achievable but requires addressing verdict accuracy through better training data (fixing `_build_qid_to_agent_map` attribution gap from D24.1), richer `project_context` per example, and potentially a verdict-specific instruction refinement in the DSPy signature — not metric weight redistribution alone.

## Mitigation / Recommendations

1. **Fix D24.1 first (highest leverage)**: The `_build_qid_to_agent_map` table-format gap silently drops attribution for projects using older `questions.md` format. Recovering those records improves both training set size and question_text richness — directly addressing the verdict accuracy gap.

2. **Implement content signal carefully**: Use case-insensitive matching for severity validation to avoid silent regression. Severity values in findings are `Medium`, `High`, `Low`, `Info` (title case) — check with `severity.lower() in {"low", "medium", "high", "critical", "info"}`.

3. **Drop severity validation from the metric**: It adds near-zero discrimination (91.1% pass rate) and requires weight renormalization. Use it as a training data filter instead (exclude findings with missing severity from the trainset), not a metric component.

4. **Keep the score < 0.4 filter but only after fixing attribution**: With 77 research findings at current corpus size, filtering 17% creates class imbalance. Wait until the corpus reaches 100+ research findings before activating the filter.

5. **Revise the 75-80% target to 70-73%**: Document this as the realistic ceiling from metric changes alone, reserving the 75-80% goal for a second optimization run after D24.1 attribution fix and corpus growth to 150+ examples.
