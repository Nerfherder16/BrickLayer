**Question**: Plot the empirical signal rate (WARNING+FAILURE / valid questions) across all 36 waves. Does the curve show the monotonic decay predicted by simulate.py, or a different shape?
**Agent**: competitive-analyst
**Verdict**: FAILURE
**Severity**: High

## Summary

The empirical novelty curve has three distinct phases — NOT the monotonic decay that simulate.py models. Phase 1 (waves 1-7): low signal (0–40%), basic HEALTHY territory. Phase 2 (waves 13-24): high signal surge (33–100%), adaptive expansion into hard failure modes. Phase 3 (waves 31-36): sustained high signal (33–85%), stable at high-finding rate. The simulation's smooth saturation curve is completely wrong about the shape, direction, and mechanism of the novelty curve.

### Three-Phase Structure

**Phase 1 (waves 1-7)**: Mean signal = 0.150. Consistent with early waves targeting well-designed, healthy components. Zero FAILUREs — only WARNINGs. The system's happy path was holding.

**Phase 2 (waves 13-24)**: Mean signal = 0.642 (4.3× increase). First FAILUREs appear at wave 13. This is when the hypothesis generator found the critical failure clusters: retrieval coverage (Q13.1), signal classifier calibration (wave 14-16), and behavioral edge cases (waves 19-24).

**Phase 3 (waves 31-36)**: Mean signal = 0.665. Sustained high signal after a probable question bank refresh or Forge synthesis reset. The campaign did NOT saturate — it maintained discovery rate through wave 36.

### Comparison with Simulate.py Prediction

| Metric | Simulation | Empirical |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Early wave signal | HIGH (first wave: 100%) | LOW (waves 1-7: mean 15%) |
| Late wave signal | LOW (wave 7: below WARNING floor) | HIGH (waves 31-36: mean 66.5%) |
| Curve shape | Monotonic decay | U-shaped / J-shaped |
| Ceiling wave | Wave 7 | Not reached (still finding at wave 36) |
| Mechanism | Saturation (redundancy) | Adaptive expansion (targeting) |

The simulation and reality have **inverse novelty profiles**. The simulation models early waves as maximally productive and late waves as redundant. Reality shows early waves as baseline checks (mostly healthy) and late waves as deep structural finding.

### Why the Simulation is Inverted

The simulation sets `WAVE_UNIQUENESS` starting at 1.0 and decaying by `WAVE_SATURATION_RATE=0.15` per wave. This assumes all possible good questions are asked first, then quality decays. But the hypothesis generator doesn't work this way — it generates questions BASED ON PRIOR FINDINGS. Early waves have no findings to build on, so they ask general questions (which pass). Late waves have rich findings to build on, so they ask targeted questions (which fail).

This is the **accumulating context effect**: the hypothesis generator improves over waves, not degrades.

## Mitigation Recommendation

1. **Invert the novelty model**: `WAVE_UNIQUENESS` should START LOW and INCREASE over time as the hypothesis generator accumulates context. A sigmoid curve (S-shape) from 0.20 to 0.85 across 20 waves is more empirically accurate.

2. **Add adaptive expansion to simulate.py**: Each wave that produces a FAILURE should increase the next wave's expected signal rate by +0.05 (hypothesis generator narrows in on failure clusters).

3. **The simulation's WAVE_NOVELTY_FLOOR_FAILURE threshold is misleading**: In the real system, hitting the floor would mean the campaign is stuck on deployment-blocked re-checks (see Q2.4), not genuine saturation. True saturation doesn't appear to occur within 36 waves.

4. **Re-examine Q1.4**: The wave count ceiling finding (wave 7) is based on the inverted simulation model. The real ceiling for a well-functioning campaign is much later — possibly determined by Forge question-generation limits, not saturation.
